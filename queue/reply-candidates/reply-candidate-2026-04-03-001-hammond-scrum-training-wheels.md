# Source summary

Hammond argues he doesn't care about sprint deadlines, velocity, or burn-down charts, and that Scrum is "agile with training wheels" that should be temporary. His conclusion is that Scrum has become an anti-productivity disease and should be put in the bin. The post generated 106 comments.

# Why reply

Mark has a structural observation that cuts against both the pro-Scrum and anti-Scrum camps: the question isn't whether Scrum works as a framework, it's whether it creates transparency — the ability to see what's actually happening in the project. Ceremonies stripped of their real function (surfacing problems, exposing bad news) are indeed useless. But the ceremonies themselves aren't the problem. The problem is that organisations use the ceremonies to perform progress rather than to reveal it. This is a genuinely different angle from "Scrum is bad" and from "Scrum is good if done right."

# Draft reply

The sprint deadline thing is interesting because it reveals what the ceremonies are actually for.

If you're using sprint reviews to prove that things are going well, they become a performance. If you're using them to find out what's actually going on — including the things that aren't going well — they become something different.

The retrospective that produces a list of polite improvements and no awkward conversations is training wheels. The retrospective where someone says "we've been blocked on this for three sprints and nobody above us wants to hear it" is empirical process.

The problem isn't Scrum. The problem is that most organisations adopt the ceremonies and then use them to suppress the information the ceremonies are supposed to surface.

You end up with a team that is visibly doing agile and genuinely hiding from what the project is telling them.
