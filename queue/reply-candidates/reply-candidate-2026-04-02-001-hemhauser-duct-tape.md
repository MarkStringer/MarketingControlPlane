# Source summary

Hemhauser argues that PMs are underappreciated — they turn vague requirements into clarity, serve as "the duct tape holding it all together," and their best work is invisible. 183 likes, 150 comments.

# Why reply

The duct tape metaphor is good but undersells what's distinctive about the PM role. Mark has a structural argument: the PM is the one person with no skin in the game on either side, which is why they can see clearly — but they bear the social cost of saying it out loud. And when they get it wrong, repair is part of the job.

# Draft reply

"Duct tape" is interesting — because duct tape holds things together quietly. It's invisible when it's working.

I think the job is something more active than that.

Both the team and the sponsors have skin in the game in a way the PM doesn't. The team generated the bad news — it reflects on their work. The sponsors funded it — they've told people it's going to work. Both have every reason to soften it, delay it, ask for more data.

The PM is the one person who doesn't have a direct stake in the news being something other than what it is.

Which means they can see it clearly.

But they bear the social cost of saying it out loud.

And they'll get it wrong about half the time. The call that seemed right wasn't. The no that should have been a yes.

So the other half of the job — the invisible half — is repairing the relationship when that happens.

Maybe that is duct tape. Keeping things holding together, even when they're rattled.
