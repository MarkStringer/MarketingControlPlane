# Source summary

Justin Mecham cites an HBR statistic that 67% of plans fail due to poor execution. He frames the solution as building "robust execution frameworks," ensuring alignment with strategic objectives, and cultivating adaptability. The post uses Sun Tzu ("strategy without tactics is the slowest route to victory") to argue that brilliant strategy means nothing without proper implementation.

# Why reply

The "poor execution" frame is itself the problem. If most plans fail when executed, the most obvious hypothesis is that the plans were wrong — not that the teams executing them were incompetent. Attributing failure to execution when the plan was built on false assumptions just adds blame to a broken situation. Mark's book directly addresses this: a plan is a bet about what will happen, and most bets are wrong. The harder conversation isn't "how do we execute better?" but "was this plan ever serious?"

# Draft reply

The stat is worth questioning. If 67% of plans fail during execution, the most obvious hypothesis isn't "bad execution" — it's "bad plan."

A plan is a bet about what will happen. Most bets are wrong, especially about complex things that haven't been done before. That's not an execution problem — it's a forecast problem.

Telling teams to execute better when the plan was built on false assumptions just adds blame to an already broken situation. The harder conversation happens before execution: is this plan a serious attempt to model reality, or is it a document that makes stakeholders comfortable?

Deliver the possible, not the fantasy.
