# Source summary

Ben Oastler asks whether scope creep is ever a good thing, arguing that in data analytics projects, frequent requirement changes can be beneficial because they show the team is "constantly questioning what we are measuring." A commenter adds nuance: the problem isn't change itself but unmanaged change — decisions made without transparent communication to the engineering team. The post's position is that evolving scope, when accompanied by communication, represents productive learning.

# Why reply

The argument that scope creep can be "good" deserves a structural pushback. Mark's angle: scope creep is almost always a symptom — of an underbaked spec, a client who hasn't fully thought through what they want, or a PM who said yes when they should have said "what does this mean for the timeline?" Calling it good because it reflects evolving understanding gives the process too much credit. The word "creep" is doing real work here: things that creep don't announce themselves. That's the problem.

# Draft reply

Scope creep is almost always a symptom. Either the original scope was more aspiration than analysis, the client hasn't fully worked out what they want, or someone said yes when they should have said "what does this mean for the delivery date?"

Framing constant requirement changes as productive questioning gives the process too much credit. The useful version is simpler: scope change is fine, as long as you price it in — in time, cost, and resource terms — every single time. When you do that, "creep" stops being the right word. You're not creeping, you're making explicit decisions with known tradeoffs.

The word "creep" already contains the problem. Things that creep don't announce themselves.
