"If a leader has accountability without real decision authority, both they — and the business — are set up to fail."

Correct. But the structural fix has a ceiling.

Authority works for decisions you can identify in advance. Most org redesign efforts address exactly that: mapping known decision types to the people best placed to make them. Useful, worth doing.

The decisions that actually kill projects are the ones nobody identified during planning. A key assumption turns out to be wrong. A dependency the project was built on does not exist the way the plan assumed. A technical constraint surfaces mid-execution that changes what is possible.

No structure allocates authority over decisions that have not yet been recognised. The org chart was designed for a version of the project that is imaginary. The actual project, which is different, will produce situations that fall between the boxes regardless of how carefully those boxes were drawn.

The redesign that helps is not just reassigning authority over the anticipated decisions. It is building systems that surface unanticipated decisions early enough for anyone to act on them.
