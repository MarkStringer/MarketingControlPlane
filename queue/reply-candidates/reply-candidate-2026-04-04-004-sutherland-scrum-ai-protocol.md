# Source summary

Jeff Sutherland, co-creator of Scrum, posted about building self-organizing AI Scrum teams on OpenClaw (an open-source AI agent framework) and asserted: "SCRUM IS AN AI PROTOCOL. IT ALWAYS HAS BEEN." He also published an April 1, 2026 white paper "Trust But Verify: Eliminating Action Hallucination in AI Agent Systems." The post had 15 comments at time of indexing. The claim is that Scrum's transparency-inspection-adaptation loop maps naturally onto AI agent architectures, and that Scrum was always fundamentally about information processing.

# Why reply

Sutherland's framing is bold and generates attention, but it slides past the hard part. Scrum's transparency-inspection-adaptation loop was designed around *human* feedback — and the bottleneck in that loop was never information processing speed. It was the social and political willingness to surface, say, and act on what the inspection revealed. The stand-up didn't fail because the team couldn't aggregate status updates fast enough. It failed (when it failed) because people didn't feel safe saying what was actually happening. The retrospective didn't produce change because the data was hard to process — it didn't produce change because the team couldn't have the conversation that the data pointed toward.

"Scrum is an AI protocol" implies the hard problem was always computation. The book's argument is that the hard problem was always human: status transactions, political risk, the personal cost of saying the uncomfortable thing to the person who funded the project. Making the information processing faster doesn't change that. If anything, more data gives more material for the people who don't want to hear the bad news to generate counter-arguments.

Mark has a specific, grounded disagreement with Sutherland's framing — not a dismissal of AI in Scrum, but a precise claim about where the bottleneck actually sits.

# Draft reply

"Scrum is an AI protocol" is an interesting claim because it locates the hard problem in information processing.

But the empirical process — transparency, inspection, adaptation — was designed to solve a different problem. The bottleneck in a stand-up isn't aggregating status updates. The bottleneck is whether people will say what's actually happening, to the people who need to hear it, knowing those people funded the project and have already told their seniors it's going well.

That's not a computational problem. It's a social and political one.

A retrospective doesn't fail because the team can't analyse their velocity. It fails because the thing the data points toward — the conversation someone needs to have with someone who doesn't want to have it — doesn't happen.

AI can process the signals faster. It can surface patterns the team missed. But it can't change the fact that the person who needs to hear the bad news is the same person who has the most reason not to.

Scrum was always trying to make that conversation possible. That part of the problem hasn't moved.
