# Source summary

Petri Malmelin identifies three reasons large projects fail: unrealistic planning (misalignment between scope, timelines, effort, and available resources), poor communication (stakeholders not effectively engaged, creating misunderstandings), and unclear roles and responsibilities. He concludes: "Successful project is the one where people change their thinking and behaviour."

# Why reply

The three reasons are real but they're symptoms, not causes. The upstream cause is structural: large projects commit to detailed plans and resource allocations at the moment of maximum ignorance — before anyone has built the thing. The plan looks solid because it has to look solid. When the guesses embedded in it turn out to be wrong (as they always do), the planning gap doesn't just affect timelines: it corrupts communication (everyone is managing to a fiction) and blurs roles (because the actual work is different from what was specified). Fixing planning, comms, and roles at the symptom level leaves the root cause untouched.

# Draft reply

These are all real. But they're symptoms.

The root cause is earlier: large projects commit to detailed plans and resource allocations at the moment of maximum ignorance — before anyone has actually built the thing. The plan looks solid. It has to. But it's made of guesses dressed up as facts.

When those guesses are wrong, the planning gap doesn't just hit timelines. It corrupts communication (everyone is managing to a fiction) and muddies roles (because the work turns out to be different from what was specified).

Better upfront planning doesn't fix this. Acknowledging that the plan is a bet — and building in learning loops so you can update the bet as you learn — does.

# Grounding

- "All projects are swamps" — the messiness isn't a management failure; it's a structural feature of working in uncertainty.
