# Source summary

Dalmijn argues that when teams say "sprint goals don't work for us," it's rarely the sprint goals themselves that are the problem. The inability to set sprint goals is a symptom of deeper organisational issues: teams working on too many unrelated things at once, component team structures that leave teams dependent on others, and an inability to decide and focus. The post had 77 comments.

# Why reply

Dalmijn's diagnosis is correct but stops one step short. The reason teams can't set sprint goals is often not just organisational structure — it's that nobody knows what the project is actually trying to be right now. The idea value stream and the product value stream are pulling in different directions and nobody has named that. Teams end up with a list of backlog items because a clear goal would require someone to make a choice that reveals the project's real state. Mark can add the "what is it?" question as the prior problem that sprint goals are failing to answer.

# Draft reply

This rings true and I'd add one more layer.

Teams often can't set sprint goals because nobody above them has answered the prior question: what is this project right now?

The sprint goal is supposed to be strategic — make users' lives better in some direction. But if the project is pulled between two or three different ideas of what it's for, the team defaults to tasks. Tasks are safe. Tasks don't require anyone to admit that the direction is unclear.

Taiichi Ohno talks about always asking "what is it?" Not what are we doing, but what is the thing we're building, and does that still match what we said we were building?

The sprint goal only works when someone has answered that question — and is willing to say it out loud even if the answer has changed since last quarter.
