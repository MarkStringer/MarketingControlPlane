# Source summary

Petri Malmelin identifies three top reasons large projects fail: unrealistic project planning (scope, timelines, resources misaligned due to pressure from leadership), poor communication (normal corporate comms lost in noise), and unclear roles and responsibilities. He frames these as competence failures.

# Why reply

"Unrealistic project planning" is treated as a skill gap when it is more often a structural outcome. Honest planning produces unacceptable answers — timelines too long, costs too high, scope too narrow — so the plan gets negotiated upward until it says what's needed for approval. This is not a planning failure. It is the result of bad news not being allowed to be data. The reframe turns a competence diagnosis into a systemic one.

# Draft reply

Unrealistic project planning is usually framed as a competence problem. It's more often a structural one. Honest planning produces answers people don't want to hear — longer timelines, more resources, narrower scope. So the plan gets negotiated until it says what's needed for approval.

The plan that gets approved is the plan that says the right thing, not the right plan. Bad news is data. But data that threatens the project doesn't get to be data until it's too late.
