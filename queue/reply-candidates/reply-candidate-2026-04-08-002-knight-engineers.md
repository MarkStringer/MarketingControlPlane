# Source summary

Jason Knight argues that slow delivery is not an engineer effort problem but a systemic one: too much tech debt, cross-cutting dependencies, context switching, large batch cycles, excess process, and misalignment between engineering and business. The remedy, he implies, is fixing these structural problems rather than applying pressure.

# Why reply

Mark agrees with the diagnosis but wants to push on the implicit treatment. Every proposed fix for structural problems is itself a project, and projects get stuck in the same swamp. Sometimes the real answer is not to fix the system but to scope down radically what the system is being asked to do.

# Draft reply

Jason, the diagnosis is right. These are structural problems, not effort problems.

But I'd push on one thing: the systemic issues you list — dependencies, debt, context switching — don't get resolved just by naming them. Every attempted fix is itself a project, and projects get stuck in the same swamp.

Sometimes the answer isn't to fix the system but to ask a different question: what can we actually deliver through this tangle right now? Not what we'd deliver if the tech debt were gone. Not what we'd deliver if the dependencies were clean. What's possible given what we have.

"Why can't the engineers just work harder" is the wrong question. But so is "how do we fix all of these structural problems at once." The useful question is: what can we actually ship through these constraints, and is that enough to matter.

# Grounding

- "All projects are swamps" is a core metaphor in *Delivering the Impossible* — complexity and dependencies are the swamp, not an aberration.
- The scoping-down move ("deliver the possible") is the practical response when structural fixes are themselves uncertain.
