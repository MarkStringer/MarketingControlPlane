# Source summary

Gavin Hildick argues that most project managers set themselves up for failure by accepting scope additions without pushback. His three-part solution: say no more often, define scope explicitly from the start, and communicate risks and delays proactively. He uses a personal story of a project that led to burnout and client dissatisfaction because he kept saying yes.

# Why reply

The advice — say no, define scope upfront — treats the initial scope as the truth and scope changes as the enemy. But scope changes are usually information about what the project actually needs to be. The real problem is treating an early guess made with incomplete information as a settled fact. The project is a bet, and when the bet needs updating, "no" isn't the answer — using the new information is.

# Draft reply

There's a structural thing here that saying no doesn't fix.

Early scope changes are often information about what the project actually needs to be. The clients aren't being difficult — they're learning. And what they're learning is usually useful.

The problem isn't that the PM said yes too often. The problem is that the initial scope was treated as settled fact rather than an early guess made with incomplete information. The project is a bet. The scope change is the project telling you the bet needs updating.

That's a different problem. And the fix is different too.
