The distinction is useful but both share the same failure mode.

A strategy, once committed to slides, papers, and steering committee approval, stops being a logic and starts being a position. People who helped design it are invested in it. People who approved it are accountable to it. The original reasoning gets defended regardless of what reality says.

Planning is easier to attack because plans are obviously finite and often wrong. Strategy is harder to attack because the failures are less traceable. "Our strategy was sound, the execution was poor" is a sentence that can survive almost any outcome.

The project is a bet, whether you call it a strategy or a plan. The problem isn't which word you use. It's treating a guess as a commitment.
