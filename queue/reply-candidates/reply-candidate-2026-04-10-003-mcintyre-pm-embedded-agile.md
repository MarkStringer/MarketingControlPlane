# Source summary

John McIntyre challenges the belief that project managers should maintain distance from agile teams. He argues that PMs should be embedded within teams, offering seven specific value-add roles (sounding board, risk identification, dependency tracking, service agreements, external knowledge, transparency through data, incident management). His central claim: "The best delivery environment is one where teams collaborate — and that team includes PMs."

# Why reply

McIntyre is right about the value of embedded PMs, but he doesn't explain why the separation exists in the first place — and it's not a methodological misunderstanding. It's structural. Organisations keep PMs at arms' length because they want a reporter, not a participant. An embedded PM will naturally start advocating for the team. Most organisations haven't decided whether they want a neutral observer or a genuine delivery partner, so they default to the distanced role. Until that structural choice is made explicit, the debate about embedded vs. separate is just a methodology argument papering over a governance question.

# Draft reply

This is a real tension. The reason PMs get kept separate isn't usually a misunderstanding of agile — it's that organisations often want a reporter, not a participant. Someone who will tell management what's happening without being captured by the team's perspective.

An embedded PM will naturally start advocating for the team. Which is right. But it also changes who the PM is actually working for.

Most organisations haven't decided whether they want a neutral observer or a genuine delivery partner. Until they do, PMs will keep getting the distanced role by default — not because anyone read the wrong book, but because the incentives point that way.
