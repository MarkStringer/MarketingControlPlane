# Why reply

The OPM framing is revealing: they've called it a "skills gap." But what skills, exactly? If they mean the ability to manage a Gantt chart and run a steering committee, that's one kind of gap. But what makes federal software projects hard isn't usually a shortage of process knowledge. It's the swamp — the stakeholder ecology around large government programmes, where multiple agencies, political pressures, procurement rules, contractor relationships, and public accountability all interact. You can import 250 certified PMs and still not get closer to delivery if they don't understand how to navigate that environment.

Mark's angle: naming the gap as "project management skills" could be correct or could be a label that lets the real problem stay invisible.

# Draft reply

The OPM announcement names the problem as a "skills gap." That's worth examining.

There are two kinds of PM skill. One is the visible kind — methodology, planning tools, stakeholder maps, risk registers. That's the kind that shows up on a CV and can be assessed in an application round.

The other kind is harder to hire for. It's the skill of operating in a complex environment where the things that matter most — the real constraints, the political dynamics, the gap between what was promised and what is deliverable — are exactly the things nobody wants to put in the status report.

Large federal projects don't tend to fail because nobody could run a Gantt chart. They tend to fail because the people who could see the problem couldn't say it out loud, and the people who could say it out loud didn't know what to look for.

Whether 250 new PMs will help depends entirely on which of those two gaps OPM thinks it's filling.
