# Source summary

Tyler Caskey asks whether most project managers are bad, based on experience with four PMs. Three failed in different ways: one was a "paid following-up machine" who did no actual work and was avoided by the team; one had no technical understanding and dominated meetings without contributing; one was Agile-trained but rushed past critical issues. The fourth succeeded by learning the technology, managing people, taking responsibility, and communicating simply without overcomplicating things.

# Why reply

The "follow-up machine" diagnosis is right, but the reason matters. The failure isn't following up — it's following up without substance. A PM who tracks status and reports it honestly, including bad news, is genuinely valuable. The "paid following-up machine" fails not because he tracks things but because he doesn't understand what he's looking at and can't do anything useful with what he finds. Mark can extend Caskey's good observation with a sharper explanation of why.

# Draft reply

Tyler - the follow-up machine problem is real, but I'd put it slightly differently.

Following up is fine. Following up with honesty is the hard part.

A PM who tracks every status and reports it accurately — including bad news — is genuinely useful. Bad news is data. You can act on data. A PM who chases status to look busy, or who filters the uncomfortable parts out of reports before passing them upward, is worse than useless. He's actively harmful.

The "paid following-up machine" isn't bad because he follows up. He's bad because he doesn't know what he's looking at and can't do anything useful with what he finds. Which, as you say, is why the team stops engaging — they know it's going nowhere.

Your fourth PM was worth keeping precisely because he understood the work well enough to know which news mattered.
