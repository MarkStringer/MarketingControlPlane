The confidence problem isn't primarily psychological. It's structural.

Projects are organised around ceremonies designed to produce commitment: the business case, the plan approval, the steering committee review. Each stage exists to generate consensus, not to test assumptions. By the time you reach delivery, the plan has been approved by enough people that questioning it is institutional insubordination, not rational inquiry.

You don't need a leader who has become overconfident. You need a process that systematically rewards confidence and penalises doubt. Most projects have exactly that, regardless of who leads them.

Flyvbjerg's point about leaders needing contrarians is right. But it treats this as a leadership failure. The harder truth is that the contrarian, if they exist, is usually right about the wrong thing at the wrong time, and the system absorbs them.
