# Source summary

Justin MacBale questions whether managing constraints is enough to define the PM role, and asks whether the job is simply to deliver and move on.

# Why reply

The constraints framing (time, cost, scope) is how PM gets taught, but it misses the harder thing: being honest about what the constraints actually imply for what's achievable. Most PMs can manage a Gantt chart. The rare skill is being willing to tell the sponsor that the plan they signed off on won't survive contact with reality — and doing that early enough to matter.

# Draft reply

Managing constraints is where the job starts, not where it ends.

The harder thing is telling the truth about what the constraints actually mean. Most projects have a plan that was agreed upfront, when everyone knew the least about what the work would involve. The constraints haven't changed. The understanding of what they imply has.

The PM who just manages the constraints delivers what was promised. The PM who reads what the constraints are actually saying delivers what's possible.

Those two things are not always the same. And the gap between them is where most projects quietly fail.
