# Source summary

Craig Brown's post raises "the ultimate problem in project management" — framed around defining what project management actually is.

# Why reply

Mark's point is that the problem is structural, not definitional. Even a perfect definition wouldn't resolve it, because the right behaviours depend on at least three dimensions: project complexity, team maturity, and stakeholder/organisational maturity. That gives you a cube of positions, each requiring a different PM approach. The goal (delivering something valuable) is constant; what that looks like is always different.

# Draft reply

This is a very important point. And I think it's structural.

It isn't a case of "if only we could define what a project manager was, we could just pull up our sleeves and be good at that."

Project managers act as an interface. How complex is the project — relatively straightforward, or (OMG) pioneering? How mature is the team? How mature are the clients, stakeholders, organisation?

That's three dimensions already, and there are probably more. Every position in that cube needs a different set of behaviours.

The goal stays the same: deliver something valuable to the organisation and its customers. But exactly what that looks like is different on every project, and often different at different stages of the same project.
