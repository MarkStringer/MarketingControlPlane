# Source summary

The Plan Port post argues that most projects miss deadlines not because teams are lazy but because "systems are weak." It lists twelve failure factors (scope creep, poor communication, slow decisions, etc.) and frames them as leadership and discipline problems. High-performing teams succeed by "removing friction before it becomes delay."

# Why reply

The diagnosis is right but the prescription is incomplete. Calling systems "weak" implies you just need stronger ones. But many project systems aren't weak — they're doing exactly what they were designed to do, which is make bad news hard to see. Every reporting layer is an opportunity to soften, reframe, or delay a difficult signal. Strengthening that kind of system makes things worse, not better. The structural observation Mark can add: a good system doesn't just enforce process — it makes deviating from plan visible early, and makes surfacing that deviation the path of least resistance rather than the most punished behaviour.

# Draft reply

The list of causes is accurate. But "weak systems" is the wrong frame.

Most project systems aren't weak. They're working exactly as designed — which is to make bad news difficult to surface. Every layer of reporting is an opportunity to soften the signal. A PM who says "we're six weeks behind" gets a harder conversation than one who says "we're on track to deliver the revised scope." The system rewards the second behaviour, and so that's what you get.

You don't need a stronger system. You need one where surfacing deviation early is the path of least resistance rather than the most punishing option. That's a different design problem entirely.
