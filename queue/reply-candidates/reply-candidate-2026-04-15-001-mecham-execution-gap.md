# Source summary

Justin Mecham cites HBR data that only 33% of plans are executed successfully, then argues the problem is execution: organisations need better execution frameworks, strategic alignment, and adaptable cultures. The implicit message is that the plan itself is fine — what breaks is the implementation.

# Why reply

The "execution gap" framing lets planners off the hook. If most plans fail in execution, there are two possible explanations: poor execution, or plans that were never realistic to begin with. Mecham only considers the first. Mark can point out that the execution problem often originates at the planning stage — and that no execution framework closes the gap between a fantasy plan and reality.

# Draft reply

The 67% execution failure figure is doing a lot of work here. One explanation is poor execution. Another is that most of those plans were never realistic in the first place.

Execution frameworks help. But they can't close the gap between what a plan promises and what the work will actually produce. That gap usually gets built in at kickoff, when the sponsor needs to hear a particular date rather than the date the work would give them.

Deliver the possible, not the fantasy. That's a planning problem before it's an execution problem.
