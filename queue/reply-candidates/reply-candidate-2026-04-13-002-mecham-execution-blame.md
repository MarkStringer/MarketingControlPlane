# Source summary

Justin Mecham cites a Harvard Business Review statistic that only 33% of plans are executed successfully. His argument: execution is what determines success. He frames the problem as a gap between strategy and tactics (invoking Sun Tzu), and proposes building robust execution frameworks, ensuring strategic alignment, and cultivating adaptability as solutions.

# Why reply

The statistic is being read backwards. If two-thirds of plans fail, the most likely explanation is that the planning was wrong — not that execution let the plan down. Plans made under uncertainty will be built on assumptions that don't hold. Blaming execution for that is like blaming the driver for a map with missing roads. Mark can offer a structural reframe: the failure lives upstream, in the planning assumptions, and the fix is to plan more honestly, not to execute more rigorously against a flawed plan.

# Draft reply

Justin - the statistic is interesting but I think it's being read the wrong way round.

If two-thirds of plans fail, the problem is almost certainly in the planning. You made a plan that couldn't be executed — because the assumptions were wrong, the complexity was underestimated, or the dependencies weren't visible at the start. Blaming execution for that is like blaming the driver for a map with missing roads.

The Sun Tzu quote actually says this. Strategy without tactics is slow; tactics without strategy is noise. But neither helps if the strategy is based on a fantasy of what's possible.

The fix isn't to execute harder. It's to plan more honestly — to admit what you don't know, build in the room to find out, and deliver the possible rather than the plan you wished you could execute.

The 33% who succeed probably started with better planning assumptions, not better execution discipline.
