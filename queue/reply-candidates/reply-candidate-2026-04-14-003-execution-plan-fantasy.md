# Source summary

Justin Mecham (228 comments) cites a stat attributed to Harvard Business Review: "67% of plans fail due to poor execution." He invokes Sun Tzu ("strategy without tactics is the slowest route to victory") and argues leaders need stronger execution frameworks, strategic alignment, and cultures that embrace change.

# Why reply

The execution-vs-planning binary assumes the plan was realistic in the first place. If the plan was built on optimistic estimates, unstated assumptions, or politically motivated milestones, "poor execution" is just the name you give it when the fantasy meets reality. You can execute a bad plan perfectly and still fail. The more important question is whether the plan was ever achievable — which requires treating planning as a bet under uncertainty, not a blueprint.

# Draft reply

That stat does a lot of work, but the framing assumes the plan was realistic to begin with.

If the plan was built on optimistic estimates, pressure-cooked timelines, or assumptions nobody wanted to test out loud, then "poor execution" is just what you call it when the fantasy meets reality. You can execute a bad plan perfectly and still fail. The problem isn't in the doing — it's in the pretending that the doing was ever going to deliver what the plan said it would.

The question worth asking first isn't "how do we execute this better?" It's "was this plan ever achievable?"
