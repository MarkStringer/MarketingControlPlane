# Source summary

Hannele Piitulainen argues that projects consistently fail to recognise types of risk. She identifies four categories: variation (foreseeable), foreseen uncertain (probable but not guaranteed), unforeseen uncertain (undetectable in advance — she uses log4j as an example), and constant chaos (projects where goals evolve through incremental learning, e.g. COVID vaccine development). She argues each type requires different management approaches and recommends a multi-level framework.

# Why reply

The taxonomy is useful but the third and fourth categories point to a structural limit of register-based risk management: for unknown unknowns, the right protection isn't a better framework — it's the ability to detect and respond fast. A thorough-looking risk register can create the impression that unregistered risks have been handled. That's the dangerous part.

# Draft reply

The taxonomy is useful. But the third and fourth categories point to the same structural problem: when you can't predict the risk, categorising it doesn't help you survive it.

For the log4j class of problem, what protects you isn't a multi-level framework — it's short cycles, fast feedback, and the habit of treating bad news as information rather than embarrassment. A risk register that doesn't include log4j tells you nothing about log4j.

The danger is that a thorough-looking risk register creates the impression that the risks you're NOT tracking have somehow been handled. The register covers what you know. The unknown unknowns are, by definition, not on it.

# Grounding

- "Bad news is data" — the appropriate response to unforeseen risk is detection speed, not prediction depth.
- Short cycles as risk management is core to the book's argument about swampy projects.
