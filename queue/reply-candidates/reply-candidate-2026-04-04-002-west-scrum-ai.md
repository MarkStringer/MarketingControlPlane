# Source summary

David Justin West argues that the Scrum Master role must evolve in 2026 into an "AI Catalyst" — actively orchestrating AI tools within teams, enabling AI-powered workflows, and staying relevant by becoming the person who integrates AI capabilities into how the team works. The framing positions the SM as a kind of internal AI adoption lead. This post appears to be part of a broader 2026 discussion across Scrum.org and Scrum Alliance (both ran AI certification content in this period) about whether the Scrum Master role is evolving or being made obsolete by AI.

# Why reply

The "AI Catalyst" framing is a recognisable move: when a role feels threatened, reframe it around whatever the current conversation is. But it risks losing the one thing the SM/PM role was actually supposed to do that nobody else in the room wants to do. The Scrum Guide's original rationale for the SM was to make reality visible — to help the team surface what it would prefer not to say, and to make that visible to the stakeholders who would prefer not to hear it. That is not an AI tooling problem. If the SM becomes the person who optimises the pipeline, who watches the pirate ship?

Mark has a concrete structural argument here: the SM/PM is useful precisely because they have less skin in the game on either side. They didn't write the code, didn't fund the project. That position gives them a sight-line the rest of the room doesn't have. Pivoting to AI orchestration doesn't preserve that sight-line — it gives up the one reason to have the role.

# Draft reply

The "AI Catalyst" framing makes the role sound more important. Orchestrating powerful tools, enabling velocity. Hard to argue with.

But the Scrum Guide's original case for a Scrum Master was simpler and harder: make what is actually happening visible to the people who need to see it. Facilitate honest inspection. Create conditions where someone says the thing no one wants to say.

That's not a tooling problem. It's a social and political one.

The team generated the bad news. It reflects on their work. The sponsors funded the project. They've told people it's going to work. Both have every reason to soften it, delay it, wait for more data.

The SM is often the one person in the room without a direct stake in the news being something other than what it is. That's the structural value of the role.

If the SM becomes the person who optimises the AI pipeline, who notices when the team is building the wrong thing because saying so would upset the sponsor?

The pirate ship doesn't care how good your CI is.
