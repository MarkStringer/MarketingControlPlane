# Source summary

Søren Sjøgren (endorsed by Dan Gardner, co-author of *How Big Things Get Done*) argues that over-confidence in leaders creates echo chambers in planning. The remedy is diverse inputs and psychological safety, so that problems surface early. The post quotes General Mattis on keeping contrarians around.

# Why reply

Mark can add a structural observation the post doesn't make: the planning process itself selects for confident prediction. The person who says "I think we can do this by June" gets listened to; the person who says "I don't know — it depends on things we can't see yet" gets treated as the problem. You can add psychological safety and still have the wrong selection pressure baked into how plans are built and presented.

# Draft reply

Søren, the echo chamber point is right — but I think it runs deeper than psychological safety.

The planning process itself rewards confident prediction. The person who says "we can do this in six months" gets listened to. The person who says "I genuinely don't know — it depends on things we can't see yet" tends to get treated as the problem rather than the most honest voice in the room.

So you end up selecting for confident prediction even when confident prediction is exactly what you don't want. The contrarians Mattis wanted aren't just people who feel safe to speak — they're people whose uncertainty is treated as useful data rather than as a failure of commitment.

Bad news is data. The people who surface uncomfortable possibilities early are doing the most valuable work in the project. The question is whether the planning process actually rewards them for it, or just tolerates them.
