# Source summary

John Crickett argues that deadlines are rarely truly immovable — they were often set hastily, set aggressively to hurry a project along, or became obsolete when scope changed. His prescription: communicate early and often, and collaboratively decide whether to move the deadline or reduce scope.

# Draft reply

John, there's something upstream of the communication problem here.

Deadlines often aren't immovable in the physical sense — you're right about that. But they frequently represent the fantasy rather than the possible. Someone set a date that assumed everything would go smoothly: no surprises, no learning, no scope that grows in the light. The project is a bet, and the deadline is the bet expressed as a calendar entry.

What makes them feel immovable is that moving them requires admitting the original bet was wrong. That's uncomfortable for everyone involved, especially when the bet was made publicly.

The PM's job isn't just to communicate early — it's to make bad news legible as data while there's still time to act on it. "Here's what we know now that we didn't know when we set this date. Here's what that means." That's not failure reporting. That's project management.
