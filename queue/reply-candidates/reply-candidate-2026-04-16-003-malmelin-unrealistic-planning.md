# Source summary

Petri Malmelin's post identifies three top reasons large projects fail: (1) unrealistic project planning — inadequate alignment between scope, timelines, effort, and resource availability; (2) poor communication; (3) unclear roles and responsibilities. The implied solution to each is to do it better.

# Why reply

The "unrealistic planning" diagnosis is interesting but the proposed cure ("plan more realistically") misses the structural problem. Large projects are planned before anyone knows enough to plan accurately. You can't be "more realistic" when you have maximum uncertainty and minimum information. The real answer isn't a better map — it's a better process for discovering, fast, where the map is wrong.

# Draft reply

The first reason is the most interesting: unrealistic planning.

But here's what I've never been able to square. Large projects get planned at the point of maximum uncertainty — before the team fully understands the scope, before the dependencies are visible, before the stakeholders have finished changing their minds.

So the plan is always, structurally, unrealistic. Not because planners are incompetent. Because the information required to make a realistic plan doesn't exist yet.

The usual prescription is "plan better" or "align scope and timelines more carefully." But the real lever isn't a more accurate map. It's a faster process for discovering where the map is wrong — and treating that discovery as useful information rather than evidence of failure.

All projects are swamps. The ones that succeed have better navigation, not better forecasts.
