The problem isn't that leaders are bad. It's that they don't know they're betting.

Every project starts with a schedule before anyone knows the real scope, and a budget before anyone knows the real cost. That's not a character flaw. That's the structure of the thing.

When the schedule slips, we call it bad leadership. But the schedule was always a guess. The people who signed off on it weren't lying. They were doing exactly what was expected: treating a bet as if it were a blueprint.

The project is a bet. The leaders who do it well are the ones who know they're betting and check the odds while they still can.

Calling it "bad leadership" makes it sound like finding better leaders would fix it. But the right leader is still committing before they have certainty. The problem is structural, not personal.
