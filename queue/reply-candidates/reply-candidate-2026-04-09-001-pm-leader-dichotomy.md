# Source summary

Sam Aquino shares Dr. Tony Prensa's argument that "a project manager keeps the train on the tracks; a project leader decides which train is worth running." The post argues that PMs who define themselves by on-time/on-budget delivery are competing with automation, and that organisations now hire for "project leaders" who influence without authority and drive strategic decisions.

# Why reply

The framing sounds clean but creates the failure mode it's trying to prevent. When PMs are separated from the "which train" question, you get things delivered on time and on budget that nobody wanted. The direction/delivery split isn't a ladder — it's a trap. On real projects, the PM is constantly surfacing new information that should be fed back into the strategic question. That loop is the job, not a promotion above the job.

# Draft reply

The split sounds clean, but in practice it creates the failure mode it's trying to prevent.

When PMs are kept away from the "which train" decision, you get projects delivered on time and on budget that nobody wanted. I've seen this more than once. The PM executed perfectly — and the project still failed, because the train was going to the wrong place.

The question "is this still worth building?" doesn't come up once at the start. It comes up constantly, as the project reveals what the project actually is. A PM who only keeps trains on tracks isn't shielded from strategy. They're making strategic decisions without knowing it — and without the information to make them well.

# Grounding

- "Deliver the possible, not the fantasy" from *Delivering the Impossible* — the PM must stay connected to what's actually happening, including whether the original goal still makes sense.
- Mark's direct experience: delivery-only PMs often compound strategic errors by executing them efficiently.
