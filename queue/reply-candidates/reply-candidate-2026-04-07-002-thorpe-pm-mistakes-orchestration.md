# Source summary

Guy Thorpe reflects on three early-career PM mistakes: trying to be an expert in all domains, treating transparency as a risk (hiding problems rather than surfacing them), and overusing PM jargon. His lesson from the first: the PM's value is in "orchestration" — facilitating conversations between subject matter experts rather than claiming domain expertise.

# Why reply

The orchestration point is the most interesting of the three, and it needs a small extension. Pure orchestration — connecting people without any ability to evaluate what they're saying — creates dependency on the very people whose problems you're meant to be managing. The PM needs enough domain awareness to recognise when an expert is wrong, out of date, or conflicted. The skill isn't expertise, it's the calibrated question.

# Draft reply

The orchestration point is the most important of the three, and it needs a small extension.

You don't need to be the domain expert. But you do need to know enough to recognise when the expert is wrong, out of date, or has a conflict of interest.

Pure orchestration — connecting people without the ability to evaluate what they're saying — leaves you dependent on the people whose problems you're supposed to be managing.

The skill isn't expertise. It's enough context to ask the question that surfaces the problem before it becomes the crisis.
