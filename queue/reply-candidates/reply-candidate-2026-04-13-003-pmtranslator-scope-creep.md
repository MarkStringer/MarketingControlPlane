# Source summary

The PM Translator argues that scope creep comes from stakeholders smuggling "quick win" requests into status meetings rather than submitting them as formal change requests. The advice: treat every requirement like a trespasser — "never let a requirement enter the building without an ID badge." If a stakeholder pitches a Phase 1 addition, respond with a trade-off: what do we remove to fit it in? Frame vigilance as the core PM discipline.

# Why reply

The "requirements as trespassers" metaphor assumes you knew what you needed before you started. On any project of real complexity, you didn't. Requirements emerge as you build — you learn what the system actually needs to do by trying to build it. What looks like scope creep is often learning. The question isn't how to keep new requirements out; it's how to decide which newly-discovered needs are worth accommodating. That's a different problem than the post is solving, and the ID-badge frame actually makes it harder to solve.

# Draft reply

The trade-off conversation is right — if something new comes in, something else has to move. That part I agree with.

But the "trespasser" frame assumes you knew what you needed before you started. On any project of real complexity, you didn't. Requirements emerge as you build. You find out what the system actually needs to do by trying to make it do things. That's not stakeholders misbehaving. That's learning.

All projects are swamps. You don't know what's in the water until you wade in. The goal isn't to stop new things appearing — that's not possible. The goal is to keep the trade-off conversation honest so you can decide which new things are worth the cost and which aren't.

The ID badge metaphor will make your stakeholders feel policed. The trade-off conversation will make them feel like partners. One of those is easier to sustain.
