# Source summary

Holub argues there is no such thing as sprint "spillover." Agile requires a sustainable pace, sprint goals should be strategic (make users' lives better in some area) not a commitment to specific stories, and a team should be able to meet a sprint goal without completing every story. The post had 79 comments. Published January 2024.

# Why reply

Holub's point about sprint goals being strategic rather than commitment-based is correct, but the reason teams end up treating sprint goals as commitments is worth naming. It's what the book calls the commitment-and-consistency trap: someone above the team has already told someone else that these stories will be done. The team then inherits a "commitment" that wasn't really theirs to make, and the sprint goal becomes a fig leaf. The spillover conversation is often the moment when that pre-made promise is colliding with reality. Mark can add this structural explanation as a concrete example.

# Draft reply

The reason "spillover" exists as a concept is that somewhere above the team, someone already committed to those stories being done.

Not as a strategic goal. As a promise. Often made to someone who doesn't know what the stories are but does know that they were promised by a date.

The team then faces the sprint review having met the goal — users' lives are genuinely a bit better — but having not delivered everything that was promised before they even started.

The spillover conversation is not a Scrum problem. It's a commitment problem. It's the moment when a foolish promise made at the start of a project is arriving to collect its debt.

The Agile ceremonies don't cause this. They just make it visible earlier than a waterfall plan would.
