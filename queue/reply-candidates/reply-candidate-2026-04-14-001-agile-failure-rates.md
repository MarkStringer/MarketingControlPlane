# Source summary

Brock Ray (Traust Consulting) cites a study claiming Agile projects have a 268% higher failure rate than non-Agile ones. He argues that Agile's core principles are sound but execution falls short, and calls for clearer documented requirements and stronger requirements engineering.

# Why reply

This statistic is often used to dismiss Agile, but the framing is backwards. Agile doesn't cause more failures — it surfaces them sooner. A waterfall project that runs three years before hitting a wall doesn't register as a "failure" until the final act. The failure was there all along; it just wasn't visible. This is the core of the bad-news-is-data argument: a methodology that makes bad news visible early isn't failing more, it's learning faster.

# Draft reply

That 268% figure gets used a lot as an argument against Agile, but I think it's measuring the wrong thing.

Agile projects don't fail more. They admit failure sooner. A traditional project running for three years before hitting a wall looks like a success right up until the moment it spectacularly isn't. The failure was there the whole time — it just wasn't surfaced.

If you treat bad news as data, a methodology that gets you the bad news faster isn't worse. It's better. The question isn't whether your project will hit problems. It's whether you find out in time to do something about it.
