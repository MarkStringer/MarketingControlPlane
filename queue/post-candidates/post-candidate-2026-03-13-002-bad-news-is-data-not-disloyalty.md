# Bad news is data, not disloyalty

## Hook

If a project has no bad news, I worry nobody is looking closely enough.

## Rationale

## Draft

A real project produces bad news.

Not because the team is failing.
Because reality is finally speaking.

The interesting leadership question is not:
“How do we stop bad news appearing?”

It is:
“How quickly can we turn bad news into action?”

That means:
- hearing the awkward signal before it gets polished away
- letting project managers surface delivery problems without punishment
- letting product people interpret what users are actually telling us
- having honest, low-distortion conversations about what the evidence means

Hierarchy is dangerous here.
The higher the stakes, the easier it is for people to soften the message, delay it, or hide behind status updates.

But software gets more deliverable when bosses move toward the evidence, not away from it.

Bad news is data.
The job is to turn it into information, knowledge, wisdom, and then action.

## Why now

Timely whenever leaders are reviewing project status or trying to improve escalation culture. It gives a concrete alternative to blame-driven status management.

- Bosses need trusted advisors and honest second-circle communication.
- Project managers and product owners play different advisory roles.
