Every book on project management is a lie the author knows they're telling.

Not maliciously. Structurally.

Because nobody picks up a book on project management because things are going well. They pick it up because something is broken. Their arse is on fire. They need someone to tell them how to put it out. The title makes the promise. The Complete Guide. The Definitive Method. How to Deliver Every Time. Before you read a word, you feel better. Someone has figured it out.

The author knows they haven't.

They know projects fail for reasons no book can anticipate. They know the specific situation you're in — your stakeholders, your timeline, your organisation's particular way of making things difficult — is not in there. They wrote it anyway. Because you needed the hope, and they needed to sell the book.

My book is called Delivering the Impossible.

The title is doing the same trick. It's telling you that the thing you're worried about — the project that looks undoable — can be delivered. Which sounds reassuring. Except impossible things aren't deliverable. That's what impossible means.

I live with the title because it's honest about the contradiction. But anyone reading it is expecting something I can't give them.

Here's the thing: this is also exactly what it's like to hire a project manager.

The client needs to believe someone has it under control. The PM needs the client to believe that. Both of them know, in their heart of hearts, the plan won't survive contact with reality. They shake hands anyway.

The reassurance isn't a lie exactly. It's more like a necessary fiction. The project can't start without it.

So both relationships start in a kind of dishonesty. And that's worth admitting.

What I can offer — as a writer, and as a project manager — isn't a guarantee of success. It's transparency. Focus. And some new ideas that come out of a lot of experience. A way of looking at what's actually happening, rather than what everyone is pretending is happening.

In the book, I think we discover something together. Not a recipe. But a new way of knowing what you're doing. Some ways of thinking about projects that are honest about the uncertainty, honest about the risk, honest about the fact that some projects will fail. Some will be such stinkers that you should probably run away.

Not every project will be a success. But what we've learned might give you a better chance. And when things go wrong — as they will, as they do — it might mean you don't feel quite so bad.
