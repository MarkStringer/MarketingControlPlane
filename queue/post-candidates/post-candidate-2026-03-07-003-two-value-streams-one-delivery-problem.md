# Two value streams, one delivery problem

## Hook

A lot of software conflict makes more sense when you realise there are two value streams, not one.

## Rationale

This exists to raise awareness of the book through one of its most distinctive ideas: the conflict between idea value and product value. It is softer promotional content because it nods to the book without making a hard sell.

## Draft

Before a team ships a product, an idea has usually already been sold.

That means many software projects are carrying two value streams at once:
1. the idea stream that won attention, support, and budget
2. the product stream that has to become useful in the real world

Those streams do not naturally agree.

The idea often wins support because it sounds neat, urgent, strategic, or politically attractive.
The product only proves itself by surviving contact with users, constraints, and trade-offs.

That is why delivery can feel tense even when everyone is trying to help.
The team is not just building software.
It is managing the gap between the dream that got funded and the reality that might actually work.

Good project management lives in that gap:
less defending the original story
more learning what users need
less smelling the flowers
more growing the fruit

That is a big part of what I’m exploring in Delivering the Impossible.

## Why now

Strong book-awareness post because it introduces a memorable concept that differentiates the author from generic delivery commentary while still being immediately useful to project and product leaders.

## Supporting ideas

- Software projects contain both an idea value stream and a product value stream.
- Ideas often get funded for emotional or political reasons rather than rigorous testing.
- Delivering real value usually means changing the original idea based on user learning.
- The flowers vs fruit metaphor distinguishes attractive ideas from useful products.
