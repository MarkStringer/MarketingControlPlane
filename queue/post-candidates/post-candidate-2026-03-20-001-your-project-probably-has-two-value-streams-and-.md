# Your project probably has two value streams, and they are not on speaking terms

## Hook

A lot of software delivery pain starts when we pretend the funded idea and the useful product are the same thing.

## Rationale

This is a strong evergreen LinkedIn post built around one of the book’s most distinctive ideas rather than generic delivery commentary. It directly supports awareness of the book while standing alone as a useful framing for practitioners.

## Draft

One of the most useful ways to see a software project is as a clash between two value streams.

The first stream is the idea stream.
That’s how an idea gets attention, sponsorship, budget, and momentum.

The second is the product stream.
That’s the hard journey of discovering what users actually need and building something that works in the real world.

The trouble is that the idea stream often arrives first, with confidence, politics, and a nice story.
The product stream arrives later, carrying awkward evidence.

And that is where projects get tense.

Because delivering real value usually means changing the original idea.
Not because the team is failing.
Because reality has entered the room.

A lot of delivery conflict is really this:
people defending the idea stream while the team is trying to build the product stream.

If you can see both streams clearly, a lot of confusing behaviour starts to make sense:
- why stakeholders push for the original promise
- why user research feels threatening
- why working software changes the conversation
- why “scope debates” are often really value debates

Good project management is not just moving tickets through a system.
It is helping a project cross the gap between dream and reality without losing the people who funded the dream.

That is a big theme in Delivering the Impossible.

## Why now

## Supporting ideas

- Software projects involve two value streams: the idea stream and the product stream.
- The idea stream often wins support for emotional or political reasons.
- Delivering real value usually means changing the original idea.
- Project success depends on guiding the team through the gap between dream and reality.
