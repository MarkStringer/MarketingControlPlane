# Rationale

The grammar books — and the culture around them — make people think they can't write until they know all the rules. But a lot of the rules aren't rules. And the real point isn't grammar anyway: it's readability. This post untangles those three things and lands on something unexpected: voice isn't the entry requirement. It's the reward.

# Draft

---

Grammar for people who aren't twats.

The grammar books come with a certain amount of associated twattery.

Eats, Shoots & Leaves. Have You Eaten, Grandma? Strunk and White. Fowler's Modern English Usage.

They're all a bit like: if when you were learning to drive, you had to put on a morning coat and a black bow tie. And you would fail your test if you didn't pronounce your H's.

There's something mad about that. Because *why* do we want to learn grammar?

We want it so we can write. So we can communicate. Our own ways of speaking are fine for our local group — friends, family, the people who know our particular quirks and foibles. But if we're talking to the billion people on the planet who can read English and don't know those quirks — grammar is how we extend our reach.

---

But here's the thing. A lot of the "rules" aren't rules.

You can end a sentence with a preposition.

You can start a sentence with because.

To carelessly split an infinitive is completely fine. That rule was borrowed from Latin — where you genuinely can't split an infinitive because it's one word. Someone applied it to English, where it makes no sense. It's not a morning coat. It's a top hat. It was never required. It exists only to make people feel like they can't write.

The Oxford comma doesn't even have a top hat. It has a top hat, a bow tie, and a butler called George.

---

The actual short list — the things worth knowing — is not long.

Apostrophes.

Commas.

How to punctuate speech.

Everything else — semi-colons, colons — is like a power drill. You might have cause to get it out of its box and use it twice a year.

That's roughly it. Not because someone will mark you down. Because getting them wrong makes your writing harder to read. The reader stumbles. The spell breaks.

The real reason to care about grammar isn't the rules. It's readability.

---

When I was at school, for various political reasons we got no sex education — and this was right in the middle of the AIDS epidemic. In one of our last lessons, one of my teachers just said: OK. I'm going to teach you what you need to know.

She was heavily pregnant at the time, which made it all very vivid.

She talked through everything that mattered. It didn't take long.

Grammar is the same. It's not a long list. You don't have to honour any of it — nobody is going to die. But if you know it, you extend your reach.

---

And here's the weird part.

If you get confident writing readable sentences — you might find something that you thought you should have had right from the start, but that kept eluding you.

Your voice.

People treat voice as the prerequisite. The thing you need before you can write. The thing that either you have or you don't.

But it might be the other way round. You write your way into it.
