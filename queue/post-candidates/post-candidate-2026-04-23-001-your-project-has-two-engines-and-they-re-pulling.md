# Your Project Has Two Engines — and They're Pulling in Opposite Directions

## Hook

Every software project has two engines. One is pulling toward the idea. The other is pulling toward what users actually need. They rarely point the same way.

## Draft

Every software project has two engines running at the same time.

The first is the **idea value stream**: the process by which an idea got attention, got funded, got a team. It often has political momentum. People are emotionally invested. Careers are attached.

The second is the **product value stream**: the process of figuring out what users actually need and building something that delivers it.

Here's the uncomfortable part: these two streams usually conflict.

The idea got funded because it was persuasive — not because it was tested. The people who backed it want to see the original idea delivered. But users, when you actually talk to them, often want something different.

So the project manager is stuck between two realities: the one that got the money, and the one that will actually work.

Most project dysfunction I've seen comes from pretending this tension doesn't exist. Treating the original idea as sacred. Never surfacing the gap between dream and reality until it's too late.

The better move: name the gap early. Show the evidence. Help the people who backed the idea see that changing it isn't failure — it's the job.

That's what guiding a project through the gap between dream and reality actually looks like.
