## Draft

A novelist gave a room full of aspiring writers the best advice I've heard.

Three words. *Tell me a story.*

I went to an event at a local bookshop — a published author and an agent reviewing first lines from people's manuscripts. I went in a bit sceptical. Came out thinking about it for days.

The novelist Freya Bromley was asked about writing style, which is one of those questions I expected to produce either useless mysticism or a reading list. Instead she said: *tell me a story.*

Not 'find A style.' Find *your* style — by noticing how you already tell stories differently in different contexts.

You tell a story one way to a child. Another way to your parents when you were making excuses. Probably a very particular way if you were found hanging over a body with a knife in your hand and blood all down your front.

Your style is already there. It's the specific shape of your distortions.

This maps directly onto something I keep coming back to in how we think about software delivery. The most useful thing a project manager can bring isn't a method — it's a point of view. A way of seeing what's actually happening, rather than what the plan says is happening.

Point of view is the only thing you have. Learning to use it deliberately — in writing, in delivery, in any difficult project — is the whole skill.

You're not trying to find A voice or follow A method. You're trying to find yours.
