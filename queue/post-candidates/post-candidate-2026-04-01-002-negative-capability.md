# Rationale

Connects to the show (shitty first draft, talking out loud, just starting) and to the book's conclusion. Strong standalone video that also seeds the show's grammar/writing section.

# Video script draft

---

When you sit down to write, there's a very good chance you won't know what you want to say.

I don't mean you haven't got an idea. You might have a very strong feeling that you've got something. But the exact thing — what it is, what order it goes in, what words you're going to use — you don't know that yet.

And the temptation — the very strong temptation — is to go and find out before you start.

To plan. To outline. To read a bit more. To wait until you know what you're doing.

John Keats called this "the irritable reaching after fact and reason."

He was talking about a creative response. And we tend to imagine a creative response as something detailed and exact — the finished poem, the precise plan, the thing fully formed before it touches the page.

But Keats was describing something different. He called the alternative "negative capability."

"Being in uncertainties, mysteries, doubts, without any irritable reaching after fact and reason."

Here's the thing. Not knowing what you're doing, when you start to write, is not a problem to be solved before you begin.

It is the condition.

Every writer you've ever heard of started from this place. The difference between the ones who wrote something and the ones who didn't isn't that some of them knew what they were doing and some of them didn't.

It's that some of them were comfortable enough with not knowing to start anyway.

And here's what I've found — and I think this is the weird part — the not-knowing doesn't go away when you start. But it changes. It stops being paralysing and starts being interesting. You find out what you think by writing it down. The thing you were trying to say shows up somewhere in the middle — not at the beginning, where you thought it would be.

The creative response isn't: find out what you're doing, then start.

The creative response is: yes, we don't know yet. And that's OK. And we keep going.

# Reviewer notes

- Opening line is deliberately low-key — "there's a very good chance you won't know what you want to say" — don't over-punch it
- The Keats intro comes mid-script, after the experience is established — earns the quote rather than leading with it
- "You find out what you think by writing it down" is the key line — make sure it lands with a pause
- The closing three lines are the rhythm that matters — keep them short and separate
