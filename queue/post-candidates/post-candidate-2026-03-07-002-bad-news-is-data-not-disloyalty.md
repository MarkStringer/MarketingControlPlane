# Bad news is data, not disloyalty

## Hook

If a project has no bad news, it usually means the signal is being filtered out.

## Rationale

This exists to surface one of the strongest and most memorable management arguments from the book: bad news should be turned into action. It is grounded in show/transcript-style material because it reads like a concise spoken argument and pulls directly from the boss chapter’s language about data, distortion, and trusted advisors.

## Draft

If a project is real, it will produce surprises, problems, and awkward evidence.

The question is not whether bad news appears.
The question is whether anyone can use it.

Bad news is data.

But hierarchy distorts data. People soften it, delay it, or hide it. Then leaders think they are managing certainty when they are really managing silence.

Better leadership in delivery means moving toward the evidence:
- make problems visible
- create low-distortion conversations
- let trusted advisors speak plainly
- turn uncomfortable facts into action

Projects rarely fail because reality spoke.
They fail because nobody wanted to listen.

## Why now

Useful whenever teams are under pressure, because it reframes a common delivery pain point in the author’s own language rather than generic ‘psychological safety’ phrasing.

## Supporting ideas

- Real projects generate problems and surprises.
- Bad news should be treated as data rather than embarrassment.
- Hierarchy distorts communication; SNAFU and softened reporting are real risks.
- Leaders need trusted advisors and honest second-circle communication to turn evidence into action.
