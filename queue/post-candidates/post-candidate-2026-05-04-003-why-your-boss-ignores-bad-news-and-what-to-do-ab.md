# Why your boss ignores bad news — and what to do about it

The reason your project's bad news never reaches the top is structural, not personal. Hierarchy distorts communication by design. Here's how to work with that instead of against it.

## Draft

There's a principle sometimes called the SNAFU principle: in any hierarchy, accurate information flows upward only with difficulty, because the people below have strong reasons to soften it.

This isn't about lying. It's about how organisations work. Bad news is threatening. It may require uncomfortable decisions. It may implicate the person delivering it. So it gets smoothed, delayed, reframed — and by the time it reaches the top, it barely resembles the original signal.

The result is a boss making decisions based on data that has been quietly laundered on the way up.

*Delivering the Impossible* calls this one of the central problems in project management. And the solution isn't to demand more honesty. It's to redesign the conversation.

The book calls it **second-circle communication**: not shouting instructions down the hierarchy, not withdrawing in silence, but entering a genuine, low-distortion discussion about what the evidence from the project is actually saying.

For that to work, bosses need trusted advisors — people who can tell them the true story in private, before the bad news has been softened for the room. The project manager surfaces delivery problems. The product owner interprets what users actually want. Together, they give the boss something the hierarchy almost never provides: an accurate picture.

The alternative is to keep marching toward a destination that no longer exists, because nobody wanted to say so.

Bad news is data. Data only helps if someone is willing to hear it.

What does second-circle communication look like in your organisation?
