# Rationale

Project managers have a structurally distinct relationship to bad news — different from both the team and the sponsors. The team generated it; the sponsors funded against it. Both have incentives to minimise, delay, or deny it. The PM is the one person with no direct stake in the news being something other than what it is. That's what makes the role possible. But it also means the PM bears the social cost of saying it out loud — and gets it wrong about half the time. The repair is as much the job as the diagnosis.

This is a structural argument about why the PM role exists, not just a list of PM skills. It's arguable, distinctive, and grounded in real experience.

# Draft

Project managers have a different professional relationship to bad news.

Both the team and the sponsors have skin in the game in a way the PM doesn't. The team generated the bad news — it reflects on their work, their estimates, their decisions. The sponsors funded it — they've told people it's going to work. Both have every reason to soften it, delay it, ask for more data.

The PM is the one person who doesn't have a direct stake in the news being something other than what it is.

Which means they can see it clearly.

But they bear the social cost of saying it out loud.

I was at big-room planning once. I'd been hearing all day that the interim solution was dead — user research had shown it wouldn't work for a substantial proportion of users. At the wrap-up, I said: "The main thing that's come out of today is that the interim solution is dead."

The people who'd spent all day telling me it was dead reacted as if I'd said something outrageous.

"We can't say that yet."
"We need more data."

I felt ever so slightly exposed.

But collecting more data doesn't change what the data already says.

And here's the other thing. You'll get it wrong about half the time. The call that seemed right wasn't. The no that should have been a yes.

So part of the job — the invisible part — is repairing the relationship when that happens. Going back to the person you called it wrong with and acknowledging it.

Keeping things holding together, even when they're rattled.
