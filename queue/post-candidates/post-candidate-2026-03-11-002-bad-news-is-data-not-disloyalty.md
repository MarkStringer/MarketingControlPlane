# Bad news is data, not disloyalty

## Hook

If a project has no bad news, I worry the bad news has just learned to dress well.

## Rationale

This is the clearest show/transcript-style candidate because it reads like a spoken argument from chapter material and uses the author's distinctive formulation 'bad news is data'. It is practical, leadership-relevant, and specific to the manuscript's stance on hierarchy and communication distortion.

## Draft

If a software project is real, it will generate surprises, problems, and bad news.

That should not be shocking. It should be expected.

The dangerous move is when leaders treat bad news as embarrassment, disloyalty, or incompetence. That is how teams learn to soften it, hide it, or deliver it too late to be useful.

A better response is: bad news is data.

Data about delivery risk.
Data about user reality.
Data about where the plan has collided with the world.

The job then is not merely to receive the data, but to turn it into information, knowledge, wisdom, and action.

That usually requires a calmer kind of conversation than status theatre allows. Not broadcasting certainty. Not punishing the messenger. Not pretending the dashboard is the project.

The project manager and product owner matter here because they help leaders see different kinds of reality: what is becoming difficult to deliver, and what may not be valuable enough to keep.

Projects go better when bosses move toward the evidence instead of away from it.

## Why now

Relevant whenever teams are under delivery pressure and leaders are tempted to demand certainty instead of evidence. It also aligns tightly with the book's launch goal of discussing delivery issues in a distinctive way.

## Supporting ideas

- Project managers and product owners help bosses interpret delivery and value signals.
