## Draft

Every software project has two value streams. Most teams are only managing one of them.

The first is the **idea value stream**: the process by which an idea gets attention, backing, and funding. It's driven by enthusiasm, politics, and the ability to make something sound compelling.

The second is the **product value stream**: the hard work of discovering what users actually need and building something that meets those needs.

Here's the problem: ideas get funded for emotional and political reasons, not because they've been rigorously tested. The people who championed the idea feel invested in it. When the product team discovers that reality is different — that users want something else, that the original vision doesn't quite fit — it creates real tension.

That tension isn't a sign something has gone wrong. It's the work.

Successful project management means guiding a team through the gap between those two streams. It means changing the original idea without making its sponsors feel attacked. It means learning from users while keeping the project alive.

Most project failures aren't technical. They're what happens when nobody manages that gap.

What does your project look like when you map both streams?
