## Draft

Last week I said every book on project management is a lie the author knows they're telling.

Fair enough. But that's only half the argument. The harder half is: what do you do instead?

Here's the alternative I actually believe in.

You need what I've come to think of as a bi-logic. Two things that look contradictory, held at the same time:

1. Enough consistency and principle to keep a team pointed in a useful direction.
2. Enough commitment to uncertainty, negotiation, and change that you don't destroy the project trying to protect the plan.

Most PM frameworks pick one and pretend the other doesn't exist. They give you structure and call it certainty. Or they give you flexibility and call it method.

The honest version looks more like this:
— Some value rather than all value.
— Possible outcomes rather than exact matches.
— Short, evidence-driven steps rather than fantasies of certainty.

And crucially: bosses need to stop avoiding bad news. Not because they're bad people, but because hierarchy distorts communication. The SNAFU principle is real — bad news gets softened, filtered, and quietly dropped before it reaches the people who need it.

Bad news from a project isn't embarrassment. It's data. It's the project telling you where reality diverges from the plan.

The job isn't to make the bad news go away. It's to turn it into action.

That's what I tried to write honestly about in Delivering the Impossible — not a complete guide, not a certainty machine. A set of better ways of seeing.
