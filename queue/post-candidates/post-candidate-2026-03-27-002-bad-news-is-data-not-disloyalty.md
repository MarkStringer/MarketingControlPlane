# Draft

---

If your project has no bad news, that's not good news.

It probably means the bad news is there — it's just not reaching you.

---

Most managers teach their teams to hide bad news. Sometimes on purpose, but mostly by accident. They just give out these signals:

- They get angry when anyone mentions problems, or bad news.
- They suggest that people are being overly pessimistic.
- They ask for specific details - when they aren't forthcoming, they dismiss the concern

These kinds of responses build a culture where the truth arrives too late to be useful, or not at all.

---

My book is all about different ways of thinking. It's about using different ways of thinking to be smarter about the problems bosses and team members experience when they try to deliver projects. 

Bad news from a project is not a sign that something has gone wrong with your team. It's actually a sign of life. It's a sign that the project is happening. If there's no bad news, the project is either stone dead or everyone is lying about the project.

Bad news is data.

Data about what problems the project is actually discovering. Data about where the gaps are between the plan and the world. Data that can reveal threats while you still have room to move. If only the team can communicate the problems soon enough, everybody can give solving the problems their best shot.

The project that reports its bad news transparently and quickly isn't a project in trouble. It's a project that has contact with reality.

---

But why is this so hard to do? For the team to report the problems? For the boss to listen?

The SNAFU principle — Situation Normal, All Fucked Up — describes what happens to information as it passes through hierarchies. The higher up you are, the more filtered your information is. People soften things. They keep quiet. They wait until everything is actually on fire before they say anything. By which point of course, it's usually too late.

*The Emperor's New Clothes* is Hans Christian Andersen's take on the same story. Looking at it from the point of view of management, the one thing you want to ask is - who is advising the emperor? There was nobody who could just let him know he was in the altogether?

---

What the bosses on a project need is the kind of trusted relationships that the naked Emperor didn't have.

Bosses need someone who will tell them what is actually happening — not what they want to hear, not what looks good in a status report. A trusted advisor. Someone who knows what the news is good and bad, and has permission to deliver it without it being treated as a threat.

A project manager who knows what they're doing is doing this. Not Gantt charts. Not RAID logs. They're working to become trusted advisors. They're trying to open a low-distortion channel. They are trying to say the thing that needs saying.

---

Bad news shouldn't be something you fear. You should expect it from any real project.

It's a sign that something is happening — and a chance to fix things before it's too late.
