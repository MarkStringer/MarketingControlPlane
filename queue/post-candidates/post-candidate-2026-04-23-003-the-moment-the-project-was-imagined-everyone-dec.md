## Draft

The moment someone imagines a software project, something strange happens: in their mind, it already exists. Fully formed. On time. Exactly as pictured.

The project team's job, from that point on, is basically just to confirm it.

This is what I've come to call **exact-match thinking** — the assumption that the specification, the diagram, the slide deck is the product. That description equals delivery. That the biscuit tin is the biscuit.

It isn't. The map is never the territory.

And yet almost every stakeholder conversation in a struggling project follows the same script:

*Why isn't it done yet? It's all written down. We've been talking about it for months.*

The discomfort isn't stubbornness. It's something deeper: people unconsciously favour symmetrical thinking. All. Same. Forever. Exact match. It's how the brain makes sense of the world.

So when the project turns out to be partial, different, time-limited, and imperfect — as all real projects are — it feels like something has gone wrong. Someone has failed. Someone didn't try hard enough.

But nothing has gone wrong. Reality has arrived.

The skill is helping people shift from asking *why isn't it what I imagined?* to asking *what is actually useful now?* Working software is the best tool for this. Not arguments. Not slides. Not more specification. The real thing, in the world, doing something.

A small real thing is more valuable than a perfect description of an imagined one.

Deliver the possible, not the fantasy.
