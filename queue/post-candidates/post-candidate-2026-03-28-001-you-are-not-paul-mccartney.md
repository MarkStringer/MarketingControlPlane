# Rationale

A sharp, funny argument against cargo-culting the habits of exceptional people. The McCartney line is the post — everything else earns it. Relevant to anyone who has used "but X didn't do Y" as a reason to avoid learning craft. Connects to the book's core argument that writing is learnable.

# Draft

---

Paul McCartney didn't learn to read music because he was afraid it would interfere with his composing gifts.

This is a reasonable fear if you are Paul McCartney — arguably the most successful composer who has ever lived.

If you are not Paul McCartney, you will probably survive learning how punctuation works.

Most of the advice we inherit about creative work was formed by people at the extreme tail of human ability. Their idiosyncrasies get mythologised. Don't read music. Don't outline. Write drunk, edit sober.

But those weren't techniques. They were specific anxieties in specific people with specific gifts that happened to work out. The people who learned their craft and went on to do great work are invisible in this story — their competence isn't interesting enough to mythologise.

Just do it. Get better at doing it. The authentic thing you have to say is more likely to survive and be heard if it has some craft around it, not less.

- The Anschell taxonomy is background: the people most likely to cargo-cult Chosen One habits are the ones who most need to just get on with learning
