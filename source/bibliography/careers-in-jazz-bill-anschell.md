# Careers in Jazz

## Author
Anschell, Bill

## Summary
A satirical essay mapping the economic realities facing jazz musicians after graduation. The central premise: "people who want to play jazz actually outnumber those who enjoy or even tolerate it," creating a microscopic, brutally competitive economy where fewer than 1% achieve their dreams as "Chosen Ones." The essay is a taxonomy of survival strategies, each involving some form of compromise — of art, lifestyle, dignity, or all three.

## Why this belongs in the control plane
This article is directly analogous to the situation facing writers. The same dynamics apply: supply of people who want to write vastly exceeds the audience willing to pay for it; most writers end up in equivalent "classes" — the day-job writer, the spouse-supported writer, the academic, the hustler. The taxonomy is a useful frame for posts and show material about what it actually means to pursue a creative career.

## Key themes
- supply of practitioners vastly exceeds demand for the work
- economic pressure forces downward compromise
- class stratification within a creative community
- the gap between artistic aspiration and commercial reality
- institutions (universities, labels, agencies) perpetuating the cycle for their own benefit

## The taxonomy

**The Chosen Ones** — fewer than 1% of music graduates. The dream everyone is chasing.

**Gig Whores** — the largest class. Accept commercial work (hotels, weddings, corporate events) to survive. Suffer "intense phobia of open spaces — on their calendars." Conceal musical talent behind tuxedos and bad toupees.

**Epiphytes** — elite working musicians, living ascetically in "subterranean environments, such as dank basement apartments." Trendsetters and innovators, admired by peers, but "least likely to reproduce" due to economic constraints.

**Silver Spoons** — independently wealthy artists with the "luxury of disregarding their audience entirely." Pursue avant-garde work, receive arts grants on the strength of "impressive discourse" about their music's philosophical foundations.

**Career Professionals** — day-job holders who maintain their jazz identity. Insist that "mere chops are irrelevant to any music of significance."

**Survivalists** — caught in cycles of gigging, debt, dishwashing, music store work. An "air of desperation." Sometimes drift into pyramid schemes.

**Working Spouses** — outcome entirely dependent on marriage timing. Marry early and resentment follows; marry after establishing yourself and salvation may arrive.

**Jazz Educators** — university professors who gave up on performing, now perpetuating the cycle by producing more graduates than the market can absorb. Publish increasingly obscure academic papers. Book Chosen Ones for concerts and hope some prestige rubs off.

**The Industry** — labels, promoters, critics, booking agents. Often failed musicians themselves. Exploit artists through dishonest practices, undercut pay scales, and predatory digital services.

## Key quote
> "The world doesn't take kindly to jazz artists."

## Connection to writing
The writer's version of this taxonomy is recognisable:
- **Chosen Ones** → bestselling authors, major literary names
- **Gig Whores** → copywriters, content mills, ghostwriting for hire
- **Epiphytes** → literary writers surviving on grants and prizes, admired but barely solvent
- **Silver Spoons** → writers of independent means
- **Career Professionals** → the "I write on weekends" cohort
- **Jazz Educators** → MFA faculty, creative writing professors
- **The Industry** → publishers, agents, platforms taking the majority of the value
