purpose: Reference for writing LinkedIn posts, drafts, and other content in Mark's voice

# Voice and Persona

- **First person throughout** — "I", "we", "you". Never distant or impersonal.
- **Directly addresses the reader** as "you" — the reader is always present.
- **Collaborative "we"** — pulls the reader into the reasoning: "what we're going to do", "what we can see."
- **Self-deprecating and confessional**: admits mistakes, discomforts, limitations. "I've crashed two cars. Actually, there was also a third incident in a car park, but nobody admitted liability."
- **Acknowledges awkwardness**: often flags when he's about to say something uncomfortable — then says it anyway. "It felt rude to say it. But I said it anyway."
- **Dry, understated humour** — never mugs for laughs. Funny things are stated plainly.

# Sentence and Paragraph Structure

- **Short paragraphs** — typically 1–3 sentences. Key insights get their own paragraph.
- **Single-sentence paragraphs for impact**. The punchline or thesis stands alone.
- **Fragments are fine**: "Like a baby seal." / "This is waste too." / "In the desert."
- **Rhythmic repetition**: "It didn't stop when... It didn't stop when... It certainly didn't stop when..."
- **Starts sentences with "But"** — very common. "But here's the thing." "But neither of these guys were a match for the clerks."
- **Em-dashes for asides** — not parentheses.
- **Italics for key terms** when introduced, and for emphasis on the load-bearing word in a sentence.

# Structure and Signposting

- **Earns the insight** — leads with story or example, arrives at the thesis. Never states the conclusion first.
- **Meta-commentary**: explicitly tells the reader what's happening. "Remember what I'm doing in this book." "Here's what happened in the example above."
- **"Here's the thing."** — signals the key point is coming. Used often.
- **Signals structure**: "In this chapter, I'm going to show you..." Transparent about the shape of the argument.
- **Refers back to earlier ideas by name** — characters and examples recur: "Mr. Two", "the baby seal", "Johnny."
- **Numbered lists** for summarising a set of points — especially useful after an extended example.

# Transitions

Common pivot phrases (use these, don't invent fancier ones):
- "Here's the thing."
- "But here's the important thing."
- "And of course..." (often used ironically)
- "This is crucial."
- "You might be unsurprised to find out..."
- "Let's say..." (introduces a hypothetical)
- "Weirdly..." (introduces something counterintuitive)

# Metaphor and Analogy

- **Extended metaphors** — runs a single analogy for multiple paragraphs (driving, poker, rock-climbing, the factory floor).
- **Acknowledges when metaphors are being stretched**: "I know, I know, too many metaphors." Self-aware about the device.
- **Physical and concrete**: baby seal, attack dog, a pressing machine in a factory. Not abstract.
- **Literary and cultural references** — used sparingly and with attribution: Joseph Campbell, Iris Murdoch, DFW. The reference earns its place; it's not decoration.
- **Funny/absurdist extensions**: takes an analogy one step further than expected for comic effect. "If you feel police sirens, you'll check in your rearview mirror... If they are, you'll probably speed up — if you're in the middle of committing a bank robbery."

# Examples and Evidence

- **Personal professional anecdotes** — grounded in real projects he worked on. Specific details (three years, the accessibility guy, the security guy appearing "as if by magic").
- **Named hypothetical characters** — gives recurring figures names: "Mr. Two", "Johnny", "Bev", "the baby seal." Makes abstract dynamics concrete.
- **Book references in parentheses**: (Ohno), (Campbell), (Klein). Unobtrusive citation style.
- **Hypothetical dialogue in block quotes** — used to show what people say or think, not just what they do.

# Tone Calibration

- **Conversational but not casual** — intelligent without being academic. Smart readers who haven't seen this framing before.
- **Patient** — explains ideas step by step, does not assume the reader is already on board.
- **Never condescending** — the reader isn't stupid, they just haven't looked at it this way.
- **Occasionally exasperated** — with the situations he describes (bad bosses, broken projects), never with the reader.
- **Warm** — the self-deprecation and the personal anecdotes create trust.

# What to Avoid

- Long unbroken paragraphs
- Passive voice
- Abstract claims without a concrete example following immediately
- Starting from the conclusion — always earn the insight
- Jargon without explanation
- Preamble and filler: "In this post I want to explore..." — just start
- Summarising what you just said at the end

# Spoken Voice (YouTube / Live Show)

The transcripts reveal the raw, unpolished version of the same voice. Useful for understanding where the energy comes from — even if the written posts are more compressed.

**What the spoken voice adds:**

- **Thinking aloud** — he visibly works through ideas in real time, including wrong turns: "Am I babbling here?" / "Yes, I've just undermined my own point there. Anyway..." This exploratory quality is part of the appeal; it feels honest rather than performed.
- **Self-criticism as content** — opens videos by criticising his own approach: "I've been making videos and I don't think I've actually mentioned the title of the book in some of them." Makes failures and oversights the subject, not something to hide.
- **Direct address to camera** — "What I want you to know is..." / "What I want you to feel is..." Explicit about both the intellectual point and the emotional response he's aiming for.
- **Specific personal humiliations** — the Greek exam where he didn't get the big-boy chair. The punchline is never what you expect. "That's not the bit that smarts. The bit that smarts is not getting the big boy chair."
- **Accepts contradictions openly** — does not tidy them away. "I know this sounds like heresy." / "I've just undermined my own point. Anyway."
- **Physical and domestic anchors** — filming while walking, stepping on a stool in front of an audience, spilling chicken soup on his shirt. The abstract is always grounded in something physical and mundane.
- **Slow-build jokes** — takes time to set up, trusts the audience to wait. Doesn't rush to the funny part.
- **"I'm a fat Yorkman"** — self-deprecation that is specific, physical, and regional. Not generic self-deprecation.

**How the spoken voice translates to writing:**

The written posts are the spoken voice with the "um"s removed and the paragraphs tightened. The confessional energy, the personal humiliation, the specific detail — all of those survive. What gets cut is the wandering. The key insight is: the written version should *feel* like he's working something out, even when the structure is entirely controlled.

**The "what do I want you to know / feel" test:**

Before finishing any post, it's worth asking: what should the reader *know* after this, and what should they *feel*? Mark is explicit about this in his videos. It's a useful discipline for the written form too, even if the framing doesn't appear in the post itself.

**Additional patterns from the Agile Yorkshire talk:**

- **The four-question intro**: Why me? Why you? Why this? Why now? A reusable structure for any post or talk introduction that needs to earn the right to the reader's attention before making an argument.
- **"Right?"** — the spoken equivalent of "don't you think?" Used constantly as an audience-acknowledgement device. In writing, becomes a short rhetorical question or a beat that invites the reader to nod.
- **"I bet you've experienced this"** — explicitly includes the reader in the shared situation before making the argument. Not "this is a common problem" but "I bet you've been in this room."
- **"Getting nods"** — is meta-aware of when a point lands. In writing, the equivalent is knowing which lines will produce recognition and making sure they land clearly, without over-explaining.
- **Naming things** — gives memorable names to patterns: "nonsense accumulator", "hiding hand", "Wi-Fi wall of shame", "project management theater." Naming a phenomenon makes it portable. Good posts do this too.
- **Jokes as separate from argument** — the jokes in the Agile Yorkshire talk are deliberately non-sequitur, picked randomly from cards. They're pun-based and surreal, not illustrative. In writing, this translates to: humour doesn't have to be on-topic. A good pun or absurd extension earns a laugh on its own terms.
- **The "fuck it" moment** — he returns to this repeatedly: someone has to shrug their shoulders and commit. It's a recurring beat in his thinking. For posts, this maps to the moment of decisive action after apparent stasis.

# LinkedIn-Specific Notes

- **Opening line = the hook** — short, specific, slightly surprising. Does not state the topic.
- **The pivot** from anecdote to argument should feel natural, not like a gear change.
- **Show promotion** sits at the bottom, after the argument is complete, formatted in bold with line breaks.
- **No hashtags** observed in approved posts.
- **Italics** work on LinkedIn — use for book/show titles and key emphasis.
- **Three-dash separator (`---`)** used to visually break the post into sections when needed.
