# Post Voice Brief

Use this when writing or evaluating LinkedIn posts for Mark Stringer.

---

## Who is writing

Mark Stringer. Author of *Delivering the Impossible* (project management, Springer). Creator and host of *You Can Write a Book* (Edinburgh Fringe show). 15+ years as a software delivery lead. Philosophy degree. Self-described fat Yorkshireman.

He is funny, self-deprecating, and direct. He does not perform expertise — he demonstrates it through specific, personal stories. He has seen a lot go wrong and is not bitter about it.

---

## The voice in one paragraph

Conversational but precise. Short paragraphs. Single-sentence punchlines that stand alone. He earns every insight through a story or example first — never states the conclusion upfront. He is willing to say uncomfortable things, and he flags the discomfort before saying them. The humour is dry and specific, not signposted. He trusts the reader.

---

## How to open a post

The first line is the hook. It should be:
- Short (one sentence, ideally under 10 words)
- Specific and slightly unexpected
- Not a statement of what the post is about

**Good:** "I had a friend who wanted to learn to play the guitar."
**Bad:** "I want to talk about why learning the basics matters."

Do not start with a question. Do not start with "I think" or "I believe." Do not start with a statistic.

---

## Structure

1. **Story or scene** — a specific, personal moment. Could be professional, could be mundane.
2. **The turn** — a single uncomfortable thing he said or did, or a realisation he had.
3. **The point** — one short paragraph that makes the argument. Often starts with "But" or "Here's the thing."
4. **The landing** — one or two short sentences. The thing the reader should take away. Does not summarise — adds.

For show-promotion posts, add a **show block** at the bottom after a `---` separator:

```
**You Can Write a Book**
Edinburgh Festival Fringe | 17th–29th August (not Sundays)
Greenside @ Riddles Court, Clover Studio
18:20 | 50 minutes
```

---

## Sentence and paragraph rules

- Paragraphs are 1–3 sentences. Key points get their own paragraph.
- Single-sentence paragraphs are fine and often best for the punchline.
- Fragments are fine: "Like a baby seal." / "For everyone else, probably fine."
- Start sentences with "But" freely.
- Use em-dashes for asides — not parentheses.
- Use *italics* for book/show titles and for the load-bearing word in a sentence.
- No hashtags.

---

## Transitions to use

- "Here's the thing."
- "But here's the important thing."
- "Weirdly..."
- "And of course..." (often ironic)
- "Let's say..."
- "You might be unsurprised to find out..."

---

## What makes the humour work

- State the absurd thing plainly, without winking at the camera.
- Extend an analogy one step further than expected.
- Self-deprecation is specific and physical: "I'm a fat Yorkshireman." Not vague self-doubt.
- The punchline is never where you expect it. ("That's not the bit that smarts. The bit that smarts is not getting the big-boy chair.")
- Humour does not have to be on-topic. A good line earns its place on its own terms.

---

## What to avoid

- Stating the conclusion before earning it
- Preamble: "In this post I want to explore..."
- Summarising at the end: "So, as we can see..."
- Passive voice
- Abstract claims with no concrete example following immediately
- Jargon without explanation
- More than one main point per post
- Inspirational tone or uplift without a specific story behind it
- Fake modesty ("I'm just a simple...")

---

## Tone tests

Ask before finalising:
- **Does it earn the insight?** Is there a story or example before the argument?
- **What does the reader know after reading this?** (Should be one clear thing.)
- **What does the reader feel?** (Recognition, relief, amusement — not impressed by Mark.)
- **Would Mark say "that's not quite right" about any sentence?** If yes, cut or rewrite it.
- **Is the opening line the best line in the post?** If not, consider making it so.

---

## Examples of the voice in action

**Opening lines that work:**
- "I had a friend who wanted to learn to play the guitar."
- "Paul McCartney didn't learn to read music because he was afraid it would interfere with his composing gifts."
- "I've crashed two cars. Actually, there was also a third incident in a car park, but nobody admitted liability."
- "Every project is a bet. A lot of project management is pretending it isn't."

**Punchlines that work:**
- "You're not Paul McCartney."
- "For everyone else, learning the basics of music — like how to read and write it — is probably fine."
- "Learn the basics. They won't get in the way of what you want to say. They'll help it survive."
- "It felt rude to say it. But I said it anyway."
