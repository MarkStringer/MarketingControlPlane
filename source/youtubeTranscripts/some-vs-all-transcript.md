# Some vs All

## Summary
This video begins with a self-critical reflection on book marketing: making promotional videos without always mentioning the title of the book, showing the cover, or linking to where people can buy it. It then moves into a broader reflection on publicity, the Edinburgh Fringe, and the possibility of building future material or even a future book around the contrast between “all” and “some.”

The main argument is that many problems in project management come from a deep psychological attraction to **all, same, forever, and exact match**, while successful delivery depends on tolerating **some, different, for a while, and inexact match**. The video connects this to prioritisation, iterative delivery, agile working, learning from reality, and the emotional discomfort people feel when a project stops being a perfect mirror of the original idea.

It also introduces Ignacio Matte Blanco as an important intellectual influence behind this way of thinking, especially the distinction between symmetrical and asymmetrical thinking.

## Key ideas
- A lot of basic book marketing failures come from not doing the obvious things consistently: naming the book, showing the cover, and linking to the buy page.
- The Edinburgh Fringe show may become the climax of the wider marketing effort for *Delivering the Impossible*.
- The distinction between **all** and **some** becomes a major late-book idea and may be rich enough to support another book or show.
- Many project-management problems come from wanting everything, forever, exactly as first imagined.
- Prioritisation is hard because it feels, emotionally, like giving something up.
- Iterative delivery is a learning strategy, but learning is hard to absorb if people assume they should already have known everything from the start.
- Agile can be understood as a way of injecting asymmetry into planning and delivery: do one thing, for a while, learn, then adapt.
- Projects should not be judged by how exactly they preserve the initial idea, but by whether they end up delivering something valuable to the organisation and its users.
