# The Biscuit Tin and the Biscuit — explainer (2026-04-05)

## Summary

A short focused video explaining the core biscuit tin / biscuit concept as it applies to project management and software delivery. Mark traces the idea to reading about Gödel's incompleteness theorem — numbers and descriptions of numbers are made of different stuff (different "substrate"). Applies this to software: working software has its own rules, different from the rules of descriptions. The sharpest new point here is organisational: as a rule, organisations are set up to deal with biscuit tins (descriptions) not biscuits (working things).

## Key ideas

- Descriptions and things are different kinds of things — made of different substrate.
- Origin: Gödel's incompleteness theorem (numbers vs descriptions of numbers).
- Biscuit tins have pictures, ingredient lists, descriptions — but you can't eat them.
- Software projects fail partly because people confuse the description with the thing.
- Early working software is disappointing — it isn't the imagined version.
- Working software has its own rules (storage, change, delivery) distinct from description rules.
- **Organisations are set up to deal with biscuit tins, not biscuits** — with descriptions, not actual working things.

So my show last year was called the biscuit tin and the biscuit. And the idea of the biscuit tin and the biscuit is that there's a fundamental problem that when I say it, it sounds obvious, but it's something that people forget. And maybe I can't forget it so easily, although sometimes I do, because I've got a degree in philosophy — that's not bragging, it's a fact, and there is no cure.

So the idea of the biscuit tin and the biscuit is that descriptions of things and things are different kinds of things. I got this idea from reading a book about Gödel's Incompleteness Theorem. The point that book was making is that numbers and descriptions of numbers are different things. They're made on a different substrate — that's the word they used in the book.

But if you think about it in terms of biscuit tins and biscuits: biscuit tins have got pictures on them, they've got descriptions on them, ingredient lists. But they're not biscuits. Biscuits are made of biscuit stuff, and that's got its own rules — how easy it is to keep, how easy it is to change, how easy it is to store. But also, fundamentally, biscuits are things you can eat. They deliver value and deliver pleasure. Whereas biscuit tins — you can't eat them. You can't eat the description of something. You have to eat the actual thing.

And this is something that causes problems all the time in project management: people confuse a description of something with the actual thing itself. And also they get very put off by the fact that the early versions of a biscuit — we're talking about projects now — they aren't delicious enough. They don't give all the value that they wanted them to give when they embarked on this expensive endeavour.

I'm talking about software. The thing about software is that actual working software that delivers value to people might initially be disappointing, and it has its own rules of how it works, which are different from the rules of how you generate descriptions and how you look after descriptions.

And the thing that makes even this even more difficult and complicated is that, as a rule, organisations are set up to deal with biscuit tins and not biscuits. They're set up to deal with descriptions and not actual working things.
