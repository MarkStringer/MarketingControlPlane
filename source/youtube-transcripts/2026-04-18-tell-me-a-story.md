So, I went to an event at a local bookshop where a published author and an agent were going to review first lines from people's books. And I was a bit negative about it going in. But one thing that the novelist Freya Bromley said — somebody asked her about writing style and I thought, well, you know, this is a really difficult question to answer. I'm not sure there's a useful answer to this. And she said something that I thought was really interesting. She said, "Tell me a story."

And that kind of for me was a very interesting way of getting into your style — your style, not A style, your style. And you might have different ways of telling stories. You've got a way you tell a story to a child obviously. You've got a way you used to tell stories to your parents when you were making things up or making excuses. You go to where you probably tell a story to the police if you were found hanging over a body with a knife in your hand and blood all down your front.

So there are kind of different ways of telling stories. But I think that's a really interesting way into thinking about style — is to not worry about it, but to worry about telling a story in a particular kind of context.

And I think that's probably one way into finding a voice — this thing that, as I've been reading lots and lots of books about writing, is one of the things that comes up over and over again. What you're trying to do by writing lots is not necessarily get the first few things that you write published and become a bestseller etc. What you're trying to do is to find a voice, is to find a way of saying things.
