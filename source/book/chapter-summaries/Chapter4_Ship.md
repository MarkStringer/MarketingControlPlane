1. The chapter argues that projects only improve when teams **notice problems, say them aloud, and try to fix or escalate them**.

2. Teams often avoid difficult issues by falling into **“agreed activity”**: work that feels safe and busy but does not move the real problem forward.

3. The “**pirate ship**” is the obvious threat on the horizon that everyone should deal with, but that people often ignore because confronting it feels risky.

4. One reason teams hide problems is a lack of **psychological safety**: people need to feel safe enough to learn, contribute, and challenge what is happening.

5. Even when the team feels safe, senior stakeholders may still resist bad news because it threatens **their own safety, credibility, and status**.

6. The chapter suggests that delivering bad news is often a **status transaction**, which helps explain why bosses may react defensively or even attack the messenger.

7. Because of this, problems should be raised **carefully**, often in private and with evidence such as charts, burn-up graphs, or patterns from retrospectives.

8. The best person to raise a difficult issue is not always the project manager, but **whoever has the strongest relationship and trust** with the stakeholder who needs to hear it.

9. Project managers should help teams surface risks, reduce unproductive “deck-scrubbing,” and keep important problems **visible long enough for action to happen**.

10. The chapter’s main message is that successful projects require both **the courage to face the pirate ship** and the political skill to discuss it in ways that make action possible.
