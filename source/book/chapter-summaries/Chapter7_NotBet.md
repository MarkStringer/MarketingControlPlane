1. The chapter argues that some projects are **not really bets at all** because they have been loaded with so many extra obstacles that they are almost certain to fail.

2. It calls these doomed projects **“nonsense accumulators”**: a sensible risk has been turned into an impossible one by adding multiple long-shot bets.

3. These extra risks are often **self-inflicted**, such as refusing user access, lacking infrastructure, blocking environments, or imposing unworkable governance.

4. The chapter compares this to **“bricks without straw”**: teams are asked to deliver something impossible without the basic conditions needed to succeed.

5. When teams try to report these problems, organizations often block the message through **“baby seals”** or **“big bad dogs”**: people or structures that soften, deflect, or intimidate away bad news.

6. The practical response is not to fight everything at once, but to ask **what the real problem is** and identify the current constraint that most limits progress.

7. By fixing one major constraint at a time, teams may be able to **shorten the odds** and turn an impossible project back into a difficult but possible one.

8. The chapter draws on **Lean thinking and the theory of constraints** to argue that progress comes from finding and relieving the bottleneck that matters most right now.

9. Sometimes, though, the constraints remain outside the team’s control, and the project stays a **certain failure** no matter how hard people work.

10. The chapter’s main message is that project managers must learn to spot when a project is impossible, communicate that clearly, try to remove the worst obstacles, and know **when to walk away** to protect their own reputation, health, and sanity.
