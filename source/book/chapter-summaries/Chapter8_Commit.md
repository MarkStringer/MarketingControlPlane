1. The chapter argues that project management is often wrongly reduced to **keeping promises**, especially being on time and on budget, even though the real measure of success is whether the project delivers value.

2. It explains that **time and budget are often surrogates** for value: they are easier to measure than usefulness, quality, or business benefit, so people focus on them instead.

3. The principle of **commitment and consistency** is powerful because people strongly want to act in ways that match what they have already said or promised.

4. This makes project teams vulnerable to **manipulation**, because leaders or stakeholders can pressure them into commitments that sound firm but are unrealistic or impossible.

5. Software projects are especially unsuited to rigid commitments because they are shaped by **uncertainty, discovery, complexity, and changing conditions**.

6. Many project commitments are therefore **foolish commitments**: promises made even though everyone involved knows the reality is too uncertain for them to be reliable.

7. The chapter suggests that instead of treating these commitments as sacred, teams should use them as the **start of a conversation** about what really matters and what must actually be delivered.

8. To do that, project managers need to keep talking, ask **open questions**, and uncover the project’s hidden drivers, pressures, and motivations—the chapter calls these its “black swans.”

9. In practice, successful delivery often means **finding the valuable core** of what is needed, rather than blindly trying to deliver every part of an original promise.

10. The chapter’s main message is that project managers must understand the pressure to commit, resist being trapped by it, and turn commitment into a tool for **negotiation, discovery, and delivering real value**.
