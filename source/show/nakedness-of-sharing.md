# The Nakedness of Sharing

## The idea

Did you ever have that dream where you're in front of an audience naked?

Or a wardrobe malfunction — your trousers fell down, some elastic gave up the ghost and something fell out.

That's what it feels like to show someone what you wrote.

## Why this matters for the show

The notebook-swapping exercise (lines 20-22 of show-draft-v1.md) asks people to do something genuinely exposing. The comic framing of nakedness/wardrobe malfunction names that feeling without being heavy about it. It's funny, it's specific, and every person in the room has felt it.

You didn't choose to be seen. The writing chose for you.

## Connection to prisms-not-mirrors

You thought you were making a mirror — something that reflected your intention accurately. But what the other person reads is something else. Your prism, seen by their prism. And now they're looking at it, and you can't take it back.

The nakedness isn't just vulnerability. It's the specific exposure of having your distortions visible to someone else's distortions.

## Connection to tenth-of-an-arse

This is also why the tenth-of-an-arse is so uncomfortable to discover. You thought you were dressed. Turns out you weren't.

## Candidate for script

Works immediately before or after the sharing exercise (lines 20-22). The checking-your-flies beat is a natural comic moment — physical, self-deprecating, grounds the abstract idea in the body.

Could run as:

*"OK — now here's the absolutely brutal part. You know that dream where you're in front of an audience and you're naked? [check flies] I'm checking my flies right now. That's what this feels like. If you dare — swap notebooks with the person next to you."*

## Stage note

The fly-checking is a physical bit. Needs to be committed to, not half-done. Works because it's a performer being vulnerable about vulnerability — meta, but not pretentiously so.
