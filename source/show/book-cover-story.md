# The Book Cover Story

## The story

Mark assumed — and thought he read in the contract — that he would have to provide the cover. The publisher provided one instead.

It was pretty generic. A lightbulb. A computer chip with "AI" written on it. There is no AI in the book.

He asked if he could make some changes. They said: what changes?

He sent them icons to put in the cogs on the front cover:

- **A biscuit** — because of the biscuit tin and the biscuit
- **A martini glass** — because of the Martini principle: if you never want to be alone, even on a desert island, just start to mix a martini. Because as soon as you do, someone will jump out from behind a tree and say "that's not how you make a martini."
- **A skull and crossbones** — because one chapter is about never ignoring the pirate ship. The big obvious problem on the horizon that everyone can see and nobody mentions.

The cover came back with the biscuit, and the martini, and just a skull.

## Why this belongs in the show

This is the book contract / progress-into-not-knowing story continued. You negotiate the contract and win on the date format. You negotiate the cover and win a skull.

It's also a perfect illustration of the gap between the biscuit tin and the biscuit: you had a clear idea of what you wanted (skull and crossbones, pirate ship, chapter reference), and what arrived was something adjacent but not quite it (just a skull). The publisher's prism transformed your icon into something else entirely.

## The Martini principle

If you never want to be alone — even on a desert island — just start to mix a martini. Someone will immediately appear from behind a tree to tell you you're doing it wrong.

This is a useful idea in its own right: expertise attracts correction. Having an opinion makes you visible. Starting something — even badly — draws people in. Which connects directly to the show's argument about writing: start, and the world responds.

## The full admission

And I don't know why the book is twenty seven pounds.

And I don't know why there isn't a Kindle version.

And this — right here in front of you — is the best idea I had for marketing a book.

So I clearly don't have much idea how to do that either.

This is the completion of the authority arc. The show opens with "why do I have any authority to tell you this?" and builds through: I wrote a book / proper publisher / Springer / not fiction / software project management. And then dismantles it piece by piece: I won on the date format. I got a skull. I don't know why it costs twenty seven pounds. My best marketing idea is a Fringe show where I give you a notebook.

The audience has been told to trust him, and then he's systematically admitted he doesn't know what he's doing either. Which is exactly the point. Nobody does. That's the show's argument. You start anyway.

## Stage notes

The skull story gets a laugh on its own. But the richer beat is the accumulation: date format, skull. Two examples of the same pattern — you do everything right, you perform confidence, and what you get back is slightly not what you meant.

The Martini principle is also stage-ready as a standalone idea. It's funny, it's true, and it has a practical application: if you want feedback on your writing, start writing badly in public. Someone will correct you. That's the beginning of a conversation.
