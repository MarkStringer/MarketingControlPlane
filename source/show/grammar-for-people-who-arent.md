# Grammar for People Who Aren't *****

New section idea for the show. Raw material captured from voice note, 2026-04-01.

---

## The books — and their associated twattery

The grammar books are:

- *Eats, Shoots & Leaves* — Lynne Truss
- *Have You Eaten, Grandma?* — Gyles Brandreth
- *The Elements of Style* — Strunk & White (the classic)
- *Modern English Usage* — Fowler (the other classic)

There's something associated with all of them — a kind of twattery. A formality that gets in the way of the actual point.

---

## The morning coat analogy

It's a bit like: when you were learning to drive, you had to put on a morning coat and a black bow tie. And you would fail your test if you didn't pronounce your H's.

There's something mad about that. Because *why* are we wanting to learn about grammar?

---

## The actual reason to learn grammar

We want to learn grammar so that we can **write**. So that we can communicate to other people.

Our own individual ways of speaking — they're fine for us. They're fine when we're talking to our local group of friends and relatives. They don't become an issue until we're talking to a wider group.

Say we're talking to the billion people on the planet who can read English — but who aren't familiar with our particular quirks and foibles of the way we speak it. Whether we're native speakers or not.

Grammar is the tool that extends your reach from your local group to that wider group.

## The sex ed analogy

When Mark was at school, for various political reasons they got no sex education — and this was right in the middle of the AIDS epidemic.

In one of his last lessons — fittingly, an organic chemistry lesson — one of his teachers just said: OK. I'm going to teach you about sex education.

She was heavily pregnant at the time, which made it all very vivid.

She talked through everything that mattered. And she just said: **these are the things you need to know.**

It didn't take long.

That was really, really helpful. Thank you to her.

---

## Grammar is the same

It's a similar situation. It's not a big list of things you need to know.

And in the end — you can do what you like. You don't *have* to honour any of these rules. Nobody is going to die as a result.

But if you know them, you just extend your reach.

---

## Grammar helps you read your own writing

The other thing — the thing that simplified Mark's own language:

You read your writing better. You understand it better. You understand what you're not managing to say. You understand that what you're saying needs changing.

Grammar isn't just for writing. It's for reading what you've written.

---

## The actual problem the section is solving

The grammar books, and the twattery around them, make people think: **I can't write. I can't communicate. Not until I know all the rules.**

But a whole bunch of the "rules" aren't rules.

- You can end a sentence with a preposition.
- You can start a sentence with *because*.
- To carelessly split an infinitive is totally fine.

That last one is especially egregious. The split infinitive "rule" comes from Latin — where you genuinely *can't* split an infinitive, because it's one word. Grammarians imported it into English, where it makes no sense. It's like requiring a top hat to drive. The rule was never real.

**The morning coat is for the real stuff** (apostrophes, commas, punctuating speech — things that genuinely affect readability).

**The top hat is for the fake stuff** — rules that were never rules, that exist only to make people feel bad and to make grammar feel like a class signal rather than a tool.

The section is about showing people which is which.

The Oxford comma is the extreme case. It doesn't just have a top hat. It has a top hat, a bow tie, and a butler called George.

---

## The frame

You're using grammar **for yourself** — to improve your own writing.

Not to pass a test. Not to impress a teacher. Not to wear the morning coat.

And definitely not the top hat.

---

## The payoff: voice

Here's the weird thing.

If you get enough confidence writing readable sentences — you might start to find something that you sort of thought you should have had right from the start, but might have struggled with.

Your voice.

People think voice is the prerequisite. The thing you need before you can write. The thing that either you have or you don't.

But it might be the other way round. You write your way into it. You get the readability working, you get confident, and somewhere in there — your voice shows up.

This connects to the McCartney section: you don't need the exceptional gift first. You start. You do the work. The thing you thought was the entry requirement turns out to be the reward.

## The short list

Three things:

1. Apostrophes
2. Commas
3. How to punctuate speech

Everything else — semi-colons, colons — is like a power drill. You might have cause to get it out of its box and use it twice a year.

---

## The real reason: readability

But actually the grammar is in service of something else — **readability**.

That's the real reason you're doing this.

Why should you care about there / their / they're? Not because of the rule. Because getting it wrong makes your writing harder to read. The reader stumbles. The reader has to do extra work. You've broken the spell.

Grammar is the tool. Readability is the goal.

This is a reframe of the whole section: it's not *grammar for people who aren't grammar people*. It's *readability — and grammar is just one of the ways you get there*.
