# The Tenth of an Arse

## The idea

Lamott calls it a shitty first draft. But that undersells how incomplete it is.

The first thing you write is at most a tenth of an arse. Not half-arsed. A tenth of an arse.

Mark discovered this writing *Delivering the Impossible*: he'd write something, think "nailed it" — and then show it to someone. And realise he hadn't half-arsed it. He'd tenth-arsed it.

## Why this matters

The gap between what you think you've written and what's actually on the page is enormous. And you cannot see that gap yourself. You need another reader.

This is what the sharing exercise in the show is actually demonstrating. It's not just about being brave. It's about discovering the size of your arse.

## The many ways of being tenth-arsed

It might not be ready because it's too short.

It might not be ready because there are only glimpses of the idea — the prism catches the light for a moment but doesn't hold it long enough for the reader to follow.

It might not be ready because you found your voice, but then you panicked and lost it when you realised you'd found it.

It might not be ready because it doesn't flow.

It might not be ready because it's just too damn long.

## Connection to the show structure

The sharing exercise (lines 20-22 of show-draft-v1.md) asks the audience to swap notebooks and give one piece of feedback. On the surface it's about courage. But the deeper point — which the show doesn't yet make explicit — is that the writer's version of what they wrote and the reader's version are different objects. The reader sees what's missing. The writer sees what they intended.

This is why writing is iterative. This is why you need readers. And this is also why the first draft being terrible is not a reason not to write it — a tenth of an arse is still a starting point.

## Connection to Lamott

Lamott's "shitty first draft" is the permission slip. The tenth of an arse is the more honest measurement. They work together:
- Lamott: it's OK for the first draft to be bad (permission)
- Tenth of an arse: it's going to be *much* worse than you think (honesty)

## Connection to Delivering the Impossible

This is lived experience from writing the book — not theory. Mark wrote it, thought it was done, showed it to people, discovered it wasn't. The book itself is evidence that the process works: you start with a tenth of an arse and — through iteration and other readers — you end up with something Springer publishes.

That's the authority behind the claim. Not just "I read about this" but "I did it and here's the book."

## Connection to prisms-not-mirrors

Another angle on the same idea: the writer thinks they've made a mirror (captured what they meant), but they've made a prism (something that refracts differently for every reader). The gap between intended and received is structural, not a failure.
