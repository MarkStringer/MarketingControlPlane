# Richard Toye — How to Write a Compelling Nonfiction Book Proposal

## Who he is

Professor Richard Toye. Professor of Modern History at the University of Exeter. Friend of Mark Stringer. Published 19 non-fiction books (including edited and co-authored volumes). Literary Director of the Royal Historical Society. Frequently provides assessments of proposals for publishers. Long experience of PhD supervision and mentoring. Co-presenter of 2018 Channel 4 documentary *Churchill's Secret Affair*. Written for the *Guardian* and the *New York Times*. Won the *Times Higher Education* Young Academic of the Year Award in 2007. Mick Jagger has one of his books on his Kindle.

## Session 1: General Principles

### Why write a book proposal?
Three purposes:
1. It helps you **sharpen your message** and develop your structure.
2. It **presents your idea** to the publisher/editor/reader in a concise form (Concise, Complete, Clear).
3. It is a way of **helping the publisher give themselves permission** to publish your book.

### Why do you want to publish a book?
- Professional necessity?
- "Because it's there"?
- To communicate your message?
- To gain recognition?
- To make money?
- To use as a calling card / establish your authority?
- Because you want people to read it?

### Four types of non-fiction books
- **Information books** (e.g. *The Big Short* — Michael Lewis)
- **Advocacy/argument books** (e.g. *Why I'm No Longer Talking to White People About Race* — Reni Eddo-Lodge)
- **How To/self-help books** (e.g. *Lend Me Your Ears* — Prof Max Atkinson)
- **Experience books** (e.g. *Touching the Void* — Joe Simpson)

### Modes of persuasion (Aristotle)
- **Ethos** — Credibility
- **Pathos** — Emotion
- **Logos** — Logic

### The 2 biggest mistakes
1. **Not being clear in the message/content/argument.** You *must* make clear what is new, distinct or original.
2. **Excess modesty/defensiveness; the language of "filling a gap."** Assuming too much knowledge on the part of the person reading the proposal.

### Homework: identifying your core message
The framework Toye gives as homework — note this is **Why This / Why Me / Why Now** (three questions, not four):
- *This book does X* ("helps people overcome their fears of A", "reveals the untold history of B", "makes a powerful case for C", "tells of my struggle to overcome D", etc.)
- *This topic is of vital importance because...*
- *I am the ideal person to write it because...*
- *This is a book which is extremely timely because...*

## Session 2: The Nitty-Gritty

### The basics
- Length: **10–30 pages**
- Include sample material if possible
- Use the publisher's form if they have one
- Timing matters — send when ready, not before

### The substance of the proposal
1. **Title and subtitle.** Key differences between academic and trade books.
2. **Brief description** of project's scope and content. Include key selling points and your Why This, (Why Me), Why Now statement.
3. **Proposed content.** Chapter-by-chapter synopsis (probably). Limit to one paragraph per chapter for an academic proposal; keep paragraphs of roughly equal length.

### Language and tone
- Be as positive and dynamic as possible while avoiding hyperbole.
- Useful words: "key", "vital", "novel", "compelling", "challenges X", "groundbreaking", "powerful", "revelatory"
- For trade books: "gripping narrative", "fast-paced", "rich in anecdote", "dramatic human story"
- Use the proposal to give the reader a sense of what it would be like to read the book.

### Why this? (six possible justifications)
- Important/urgent societal issue
- Dramatic, moving story
- Will help people achieve X
- New (surprising) information
- To help right a wrong
- New analysis of a familiar issue

### Why now? (five possible reasons)
- Current urgency of issue
- Increased public/media interest, likelihood of reviews
- Upcoming anniversaries
- New information only now available
- Need to capture historical memory

### Why me? (author credentials)
- Experience in relevant field
- Lived experience
- Expertise, knowledge & skills
- Previous writing/publishing experience
- Media/podcasting experience
- Social media profile/email list
- Ability to make use of professional networks/conferences

### Identifying the market
- Deploy evidence of interest in the topic
- Define main audience: academics, students, practitioners, general readers?
- What is the subject area(s)?
- Mention scholarly societies, professional bodies etc.
- Be realistic but rack your brains for all possible markets

### Competition
- This is **not** a literature review
- No need to attack or undermine other works
- This is to give a frame of reference

### Author information
- For academic books you may be asked to submit your CV
- But you still need a short, punchy bio paragraph
- Give social media stats (if they're impressive)

### Dealing with referee/editorial comments
- Address everything
- Don't get mad — get even
- Accept suggestions if you reasonably can
- But don't give hostages to fortune
- Try to say "Yes, but" rather than "No"

### If offered a contract
- Consider joining Society of Authors (contract-reading service)
- Think about negotiating: indexing costs, images and costs of images, paperback rights

### Routes to trade publication
- Keep telling people about your project
- Approach trade editors directly
- Note the OUP "Impact" series
- Consider proposing an OUP Very Short Introduction

## Notes on the show script

The show currently uses **four questions** (Why you? Why us? Why now? Why this?) but Toye's actual framework uses **three** (Why This / Why Me / Why Now). The show has:
- Split "Why Me" into "Why you (Mark)" and "Why us (the audience)" — a smart adaptation for a live show
- Added an implicit fourth dimension by including the audience as participants, not just recipients

This is a genuine creative transformation of the source material, not just a reproduction.
