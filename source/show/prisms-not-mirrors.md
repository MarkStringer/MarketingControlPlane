# Prisms, not mirrors

## The Borges quote

> "Two aesthetics exist: the passive aesthetic of mirrors and the active aesthetic of prisms."
> — Jorge Luis Borges

## Mark's position

There are only prisms. The passive aesthetic of mirrors doesn't exist. You can't stop distorting what you see. Everything you write is already a transformation.

## Why this matters for the show

The opening exercise asks people to write down what they notice, how they feel, what's bothering them. It looks like recording — neutral, passive, mirror-like. But it isn't. They're choosing what to notice. They're choosing words. They're already distorting, already transforming, already making it theirs.

That's not a problem. That's the point. That's why they're writers.

The show *demonstrates* the idea rather than arguing for it. By the time the audience hears "there are no mirrors, only prisms", they've already proved it themselves.

## Richard Bandler: "Always hallucinate what's really there"

Richard Bandler — one of the notorious ne'er do wells behind Neuro-Linguistic Programming — said: "Always hallucinate what's really there."

This is the prism idea from a neuroscience/psychology angle. Perception is always a construction, always a hallucination. There is no unmediated access to reality. But the hallucination can be calibrated — pointed at what's actually there, rather than at what you fear or wish were there.

Borges says there are two aesthetics: mirrors and prisms. Bandler says there is only ever hallucination. Mark says there are only prisms.

All three are saying the same thing: the passive option doesn't exist. You are always constructing. The question is whether you do it consciously or not.

For the show: the opening writing exercise asks people to write down what they notice. What they notice is their hallucination of what's really there. That's not a criticism — that's the only thing writing can ever be. And it's enough. It's more than enough.

### Note on Bandler

The quote is useful but the source is complicated — Bandler is a controversial figure. Worth using the idea without necessarily naming him in the show, or naming him with the "notorious ne'er do well" framing which pre-empts the obvious objection.

## Connection to the "fooling/hypnotising" section

The Borges/prism idea and the Lamott hypnosis idea are the same claim from different angles:
- Lamott: writing is hypnotising yourself into believing it's worth doing
- Borges/Mark: there is no neutral — you are always a prism, always distorting

Both say: the writer is not a passive recorder. Both say: that's OK, that's the work.

These could sit alongside each other in the script, or one could subsume the other.

## Connection to Delivering the Impossible

"Point of view is worth 80 IQ points" is the same idea applied to software projects. There are no mirrors in project management either. Every stakeholder, every manager, every developer is a prism. The book is about learning to see that — and to use it.

This is the strongest bridge yet between the show and the book. Not "I wrote a book about project management" but "the thing this show is about — that you can't see without distorting — is also what my book is about."

## The political version of the idea

People who say there are mirrors — who claim to be just reporting, just describing, just telling it like it is — are either fooling themselves or trying to fool you.

There are only prisms. Everyone is distorting the world when they describe it. Either knowingly or unknowingly. Either for conscious ends or unconscious ones.

The passive aesthetic doesn't just not exist — claiming it exists is itself a move. "I'm just a mirror" is one of the most effective ways of hiding that you're a prism.

This gives the idea teeth. It's not just a philosophical observation about the nature of perception. It's a warning: when someone tells you they have no agenda, that's when you should look most carefully at their agenda.

### Christopher Isherwood: "I am a camera"

Christopher Isherwood wrote about 1930s Berlin. He opens one of his books with: "I am a camera with its shutter open, quite passive, recording, not thinking."

Bullshit, Christopher.

His view of Berlin was so specific, so distinctively his own prism, that it got turned into the musical *Cabaret*. Sally Bowles. The Kit Kat Club. The whole thing. His "passive recording" was so singular that it essentially colonised the cultural understanding of Weimar Berlin for everyone who came after. Nobody else could write about that Berlin without writing in his shadow.

The person most loudly claiming to be a mirror turned out to have the most powerful prism in the room.

That's what "I am a camera" actually means, whether Isherwood knew it or not: I am about to show you the world so completely through my eyes that you will never be able to unsee it.

### Relevance to the show

The show asks people to write something and share it. The frame is: this is your prism, own it. The opposite of the show's argument isn't "I can't write" — it's "I'm just describing what's there." Both are ways of not owning the distortion. Both are ways of not writing.

### Relevance to Delivering the Impossible

Software projects are full of people claiming to be mirrors. Requirements documents. Specifications. "Objective" assessments of progress. The book's argument — that point of view is worth 80 IQ points — is partly an argument that the mirror claimants are the most dangerous people in the room.

## Answer to "Why me?" — for anyone

This is also the answer to Toye's "Why me?" question for *any* aspiring writer in the audience, not just Mark:

Your distortions are different from anybody else's. No one else has your prism. That's not a flaw to overcome — it's the only thing of value you have as a writer.

Most people who want to write but don't are afraid their perspective is too personal, too biased, too weird. The show's answer: that's not a bug. That's the reason to write. The passive aesthetic of mirrors, if it existed, would produce nothing worth reading.

This sharpens the show's ending. "Congratulations — you're now a writer" isn't encouragement. It's a logical conclusion: you have a prism, therefore you are a writer.
