# The Biscuit Tin and the Show

## The idea

The biscuit tin is not the biscuit. A recipe, a description, an idea of something is different from the actual thing itself.

Mark used this in his previous Fringe show (about *Delivering the Impossible*). He thought it might be an original idea for about 30 seconds — until he remembered there is at least one TV series entirely devoted to the dangers of failing to turn the idea of a biscuit into a biscuit: *The Great British Bake Off*.

## Connection to the current show

The biscuit tin idea connects to everything the new show is about:

- **Tenth of an arse**: the first draft is the tin, not the biscuit. It has the right shape but nothing inside yet.
- **Prisms**: even when you make the biscuit, it's not the biscuit the reader tastes — their prism transforms it further.
- **Writing as hypnosis**: the tin is the plan, the hypnosis is what gets you to actually make the biscuit.

They are all versions of the same claim: the description is not the thing, and the gap between them is where all the difficulty — and all the interest — lives.

## The two-show arc

- **Last year's show**: the biscuit tin — the idea of the book, the description of software delivery ideas, the argument that the map is not the territory.
- **This year's show**: making the biscuit — everyone in the room actually writes something. We stop talking about writing and do it.

This is a clean arc across two Fringe runs. The new show is the sequel the previous show implied without knowing it.

## The Great British Bake Off beat

The GBBO deflation is a useful comic move: you think you've had an original idea, then you clock that Channel 4 has been running a primetime show about exactly this for fifteen series. Self-deprecating, quick, and it means you don't have to over-explain the concept — the audience already knows what happens when someone fails to turn a biscuit idea into an actual biscuit.

## What Bake Off is actually about

Bake Off is not really about biscuits. It's about emotions.

Specifically: emotions around things not going as you expect. And as you progress — as you get better — you're asked to do things you've never done before and have no idea how to do. Getting better doesn't mean getting more confident. It means arriving at new and more elaborate forms of not knowing.

And there's a second layer: you're not supposed to show any of it. When you're absolutely gutted — kicked off the show, Hollywood handshake withheld, soggy bottom confirmed — you stay cheerful. When you've got the Hollywood handshake, when you're star baker, you stay modest.

So any possible progress leads you into a situation where you don't know what you're doing and your emotions are all over the place — and you're performing not having those emotions on top of it.

This is exactly what writing feels like. And it's exactly what the show is asking the audience to do — write something, not know if it's any good, share it with a stranger, receive feedback, perform being fine about all of it.

### Connection to Impro

Keith Johnstone's *Impro* is about the same thing from a different direction: the suppression of spontaneity, the social performance of status, the way education trains people to hide what they don't know. Bake Off has formalised this into a TV format.

### Connection to Delivering the Impossible

Projects work the same way. The more senior you get, the more you're asked to do things you've never done before. And you're supposed to project confidence while your emotions are all over the place. "Bad news is data" is partly an argument against this — for letting the emotions be visible because they're carrying information.

### The insight

Any progress — in baking, in writing, in project management, in performing at the Fringe — leads you somewhere you don't know how to be. That's not a problem to solve. That's what progress feels like.

### Example: the book contract

When Mark got a book deal, a friend recommended getting the contract read by a writers' organisation. They raised a whole bunch of points. Mark raised all of them with the publisher.

The only thing he managed to change was the format of the date — from American to British.

That's progress. That's what it looks like. You don't know what you're doing, your emotions are all over the place, you perform having it together — and you win on the date format.

*(Stage note: this is a good laugh but also quietly true — it's what surviving a contract negotiation as a first-time author actually feels like. The modesty/cheerfulness performance is right there in it.)*
