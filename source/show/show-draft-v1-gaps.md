# Show Draft v1 — Gaps and Notes

## What's there (and working)

- **Opening is strong**: straight into action, no preamble. Audience has a pen and notebook before you've explained anything.
- **Three writing prompts** (notice something / how are you feeling / what's bothering you) give people different entry points. Good.
- **"Fooling / hypnotising"** paragraph is the philosophical heart of the show. The self-correction from "fooling" to "hypnotising" is a nice live-thinking moment — keep it.
- **Sharing exercise** escalates well: write → look back → optionally share → give one piece of feedback. "More brutaller" is a good comic beat.
- **Ending** ("Congratulations — you're now a writer") lands cleanly.

## What's been added (2026-03-26, update 1)

The script now has a post-punchline section (lines 26-41) that earns the right to have been running a writing show in the first place:

- "Why do I have any authority to tell you this?" — good stand-up move, establishes credibility *after* the audience has already done the thing
- The "proper publisher / not a vanity publisher / Springer" bit — genuinely funny, handles the most common suspicious question without being defensive
- The meta-closer: "this show isn't about my book" — the best marketing line in the script. Self-aware, funny, leaves the audience curious without being sold at.

The book connection is now solved at the structural level. The audience knows a book exists, knows roughly what it's about, and the notebook in their hand has the brand on it.

## What's been added (2026-03-28, update 3)

The McCartney passage has been added between "Congratulations — you're now a writer" and "Why do I have any authority to tell you this?"

- Sets up the shitty first draft idea: you don't need exceptional gifts to start, you just need to start
- Gets a laugh before the more earnest Lamott material
- Punctures the mythology around creative genius without being preachy — the punchline does the work
- "I'm going to go out on a limb here" is the comic beat; keep the pause after it

## What's been added (2026-03-26, update 2)

The show now has explicit content: the Richard Toye framework (see `richard-toye-notes.md`).

- Professor Richard Toye introduced as the source — a friend with a dozen books and two plays. Establishes that the show's content comes from real authority, not just Mark's one book.
- The pandemic course framing gives it a personal, non-academic texture.
- The four questions — **Why you? Why us? Why now? Why this?** — are both what the show is teaching *and* what the show is doing. That double function is the structural payoff.

Lines 46-52 are still placeholder headings. The four questions need expanding into spoken text (or kept as performer cues — decide which).

## What's still missing

### The four questions need answering
The show sets up the framework but doesn't yet walk through it. The audience has been told the questions exist; they haven't heard the answers. Options:
- Mark answers them about his own book (demonstration)
- The audience applies them to their own potential book (participation)
- Both — Mark models it, audience tries it

### The book title
- *Delivering the Impossible* is never said aloud. This may be intentional — the audience finds it on the notebook. Decide: is that the plan, or does it need saying?

### Unexpanded placeholders
- Line 8: `Shitty first drafts` — heading, not spoken text. Needs writing out or cutting.
- Line 10: `One inch window — two point five four centimetre window` — same. The metric conversion suggests a comic beat, but it's not scripted yet.

### The closing
- No button after the four questions. The show needs an ending. Options:
  - Come back to the opening: "you already answered Why you — you just wrote something"
  - A CTA: website, social handle, "the notebook's yours, keep writing"
  - Something that connects writing to project management — the one bridge not yet made

### Opening framing
- Still no introduction before "OK find the notebook." Cold open is probably the right call — commit to it deliberately.

### The book's ideas in the show
- *Delivering the Impossible* themes that map onto writing remain unused: bets (every writing session is a bet), swamp (the blank page is a swamp), bad news is data (feedback is data, not judgement). May belong in a future draft rather than this one.

## Source of the hypnosis idea
The "fooling / hypnotising" framing in lines 12-14 comes from Lamott (Bird by Bird, loc 1720): *"Writing is about hypnotizing yourself into believing in yourself, getting some work done, then unhypnotizing yourself and going over the material coldly."* The show transforms this: Lamott describes a private internal process; the show does it collectively, in real time, to a room full of people. The transformation is the original contribution.
